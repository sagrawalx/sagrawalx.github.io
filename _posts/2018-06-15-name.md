# Last Name

## Orthography

My last name is written as follows.

* Latin alphabet: Agrawal
* [Nagari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devanagari): अग्रवाल
* [Urdu alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urdu_alphabet): اگ‬‬روال

The orthographic variants of this last name are quite numerous. I don't think there should be any variants in the Urdu alphabet (aside from those that add optional diacritics), but in Nagari, I've encountered the variant *अगरवाल*, and in the Latin alphabet, I've seen *Agarwal* and *Aggarwal*.

In fact, all three of these Latin alphabet variants occur in my close family. My grandfather spells his last name *Aggarwal*. Originally, my father and his sisters did too, but my father and one of his sisters have first names whose Latin alphabet spellings have 5 letters. As *Aggarwal* has 8 letters, this brought the total number of letters in their names to 13. Apparently one of their schoolteachers felt that them having 13 letter names was extremely inauspicious and insisted on them changing the spellings of their last name. For whatever reason, they both changed it differently: my father chose *Agrawal* while my aunt chose *Agarwal*.

In Japanese, I like writing アグラワール.[^katakana]

[^katakana]: Probably it would be most correct to write アグラヴァール, but I don't like using the atypical ヴァ. Also, I suspect monolingual Japanese speakers would be most likely to transcribe the [Hindustani pronunciation of my last name](#last-name-pronunciation) as アグラバール, but to *my* ear this seems further from the Hindustani pronunciation than アグラワール (probably as a result of [/v/~/w/ allphony in Hindustani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindustani_phonology#Allophony_of_[v]_and_[w])), thus my preference for the latter.

## Pronunciation {#last-name-pronunciation}

The orthographic variations in both the Latin alphabet and in Nagari reflect more genuine variations in the [Hindustani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindustani_language) pronunciation as well: sometimes it is said as /əgəɾvaːl/, and sometimes it is /əgɾəvaːl/.[^variant-agrawal-pronunciations]

The anglicization of my last name I hear most often is /'ægɹəwɑːl/.

[^variant-agrawal-pronunciations]: One possible explanation for the two variant Hindustani pronunciations of my last name could be as follows. Early in Indo-Aryan, it seems that the consonant cluster /gɾ/ was permissible in syllable onsets (cf. Sanskrit words such as [*grantha* ग्रन्थ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%97%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B0%E0%A4%A8%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%A5), [*grīṣma* ग्रीष्म](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%97%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B0%E0%A5%80%E0%A4%B7%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%AE#Sanskrit), etc). If /gɾ/ is permissible as an onset, the maximal onset principle predicts that my last name would by syllabified as  /ə.gɾə.vaːl/.

    As Indo-Aryan underwent cluster reduction, it seems that the cluster /gɾ/ became less permissible in syllable onsets. For example, the Pali version of *grantha* is *gantha*, the Pali version of *grīṣma* is *gimha*, etc. In Hindustani today, the cluster /gɾ/ occurs word-initially almost exclusively in loans (many of which are *tatsama* loans from Sanskrit, but these are loans nonetheless).

    In particular, the middle syllable  /gɾə/ in /ə.gɾə.vaːl/ seems to have violated the phonotactics of later versions of Indo-Aryan. To reconcile this, there are two possibilities.  One possibility is to break apart the problematic cluster by resyllabifying the entire word, resulting in /əg.ɾə.vaːl/. The other possibility is to preserve the original syllabification and just to simplify the problematic middle syllable to  accord with the new phonotactical rules; one way of doing this is to change /gɾə/ into /gəɾ/, resulting in /ə.gəɾ.vaːl/.

# First Name

## Orthography

My first name is written as follows.

* Latin alphabet: Shishir
* Nagari: शिशिर
* Urdu alphabet: ششر

There are no orthographic variants that I am aware of.

In Japanese, probably シシル is the only reasonable transcription.

## Pronunciation

The usual Hindustani pronunciation is /ʃɪʃɪɾ/.

The sound /ɪɾ/ does not occur naturally in English, and the typical anglicization I hear is /ʃɪ'ʃɪəɹ/, rhyming with "sheer."

Very few people in my personal life call me by my official first name (more about this [shortly](#nickname)). Having grown up in the United States, I have grown rather used to the anglicized pronunciation and readily respond to it.

## Etymology: Sanskrit Onwards

My name descends from Sanskrit word *śiśira* (शिशिर), which functions both as the root of both a masculine noun[^noun] and an adjective. As a noun, Apte's dictionary {% cite apte  %} gives the following definitions.

1. Dew, hoarfrost
2. The cold season, comprising the two months of *māgha* and *phālguna*.
3. Coldness, frigidity  

The precise boundaries of the months of *māgha* (माघ) and *phālguna* (फाल्गुन)[^phalguna] depend on the calendar being used (as there seem to be several slightly different versions of the Hindu calendar), but the two month period ranges *roughly* from mid-January to mid-March (which is invariably a period that includes my birthday).

The various meanings for *śiśira* as an adjective that Apte lists can be more or less summarized with the single English adjective "cold."

[^noun]: Nominative singular: *śiśiraḥ* (शिशिरः), accusative singular: *śiśiram* (शिशिरम्), ...

[^phalguna]: In modern Hindustani, both the *tatsama* word फाल्गुन · <span dir="rtl">پهالگن</span> and *tadbhava* फागुन · <span dir="rtl">پهاگن</span> are used for this month's name (but neither is used very often, since people primarily use the Gregorian calendar now).

The word propagates into other languages from Sanskrit, both Indo-Aryan and not. Sometimes it is loaned as is (ie, as a *[tatsama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatsama)* word), and sometimes it has undergone various phonological changes (ie, a  *[tadbhava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadbhava)* form is used).

* In Pali, the *tadbhava* form *sisira* सिसिर is used; note that, as in Sanskrit, final schwas are not deleted in Pali. [Pali dictionaries](https://palidictionary.appspot.com/browse/s/sisira) mention all of the Sanskrit meanings *except* "dew, hoarfrost."
* In Hindustani, both the *tatsama* शिशिर · <span dir="rtl">ششر</span> and the *tadbhava* सिसिर · <span dir="rtl">سسر</span> occur, with the former being much more common in modern times. The word usually refers to the winter, broadly interpreted (ie, not necessarily the specific two month period of the Hindu calendar mentioned above).[^winter-hindustani]
* Bengali inherits the *tatsama* word শিশির, and it usually refers to dew or frost.[^biswas]
* Here are a couple of loans into non-Indo-Aryan languages.
    * Telugu [శిశిరము](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B0%B6%E0%B0%BF%E0%B0%B6%E0%B0%BF%E0%B0%B0%E0%B0%AE%E0%B1%81) ("cold; dew; frost; winter"), from the Sanskrit.
    * Khmer [សិសិរៈ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%9E%9F%E1%9E%B7%E1%9E%9F%E1%9E%B7%E1%9E%9A%E1%9F%88) ("winter"), from the Pali.

[^winter-hindustani]: Note, though, that neither शिशिर · <span dir="rtl">ششر</span> nor सिसिर · <span dir="rtl">سسر</span> is the most common word for "winter" in Hindustani. More common words include [सर्दी · <span dir="rtl">سردی</span>](http://dsalsrv02.uchicago.edu/cgi-bin/philologic/getobject.pl?c.18:1:1810.dasahindi)  and [जाड़ा · <span dir="rtl">جاڑا</span>](http://dsalsrv02.uchicago.edu/cgi-bin/philologic/getobject.pl?c.6:1:3717.dasahindi).

[^biswas]: I don't speak Bengali, but I am extrapolating from the fact that the Bengali-English dictionary {% cite biswas-bengali  %} only lists "dew; frost" for শিশির, and a few of the literary references I managed to dig up did seem to use the word to refer to dew. It's worth noting, though, that the monolingual Bengali dictionary {% cite biswas-bangala  %} lists all of the noun usages of the Sanskrit word.

## Etymology: Before Sanskrit

Tracing the etymology of words back further than Sanskrit is difficult, as this leads us into the prehistoric era, but there are reasonable hypotheses. Linguists attest a Proto-Indo-European (PIE) root \**ḱel*, which meant something like "cold; cool; warm" (in other words, just not "hot"...?).[^kel] It is thought that the Sanskrit *śiśira* descends from this PIE root {% cite pokorny  %}.

[^kel]: See the entry for [\**ḱel*](https://lrc.la.utexas.edu/lex/master/0890) in the Indo-European Lexicon {% cite pie-lexicon  %}, or in {% cite pokorny  %}.

This proposal may seem slightly strange to the unacquainted, but there are some general phonological phenomena that account for this hypothesis.

* The PIE phoneme \**ḱ* is hypothesized to have become  *ś* (श) in Sanskrit, while it became /k/ in Latin. Some examples of this phenomenon include the following.

    * Compare Latin *equus* and *aśvas* (अश्वस्), both meaning "horse" and conjecturally deriving from a PIE \**eḱwos*.
    * Compare Latin *centum* and Sanskrit *śatam* (शतम्), both meaning "hundred" and conjecturally deriving from a PIE \**ḱm̥tóm*.  

    This is a particular case of the palatovelar stops of PIE becoming [assibilated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assibilation) in the eastern [satem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centum_and_satem_languages) branch of Indo-European.[^palatal-k]

* PIE is hypothesized to have had two liquid phonemes \**l* and \**r* which have been quite faithfully preserved as /l/ and /r/ in most Indo-European languages. But there seems to have been substantial confusion regarding these phonemes in Indo-Iranian. For example, both phonemes merged to /r/ in Avestan, and there seems to have been dialectical variation between /r/ and /l/ in Indo-Aryan. It's possible that the two liquids had merged to /r/ in Indo-Iranian, before differentiation of Indo-Aryan and Iranian, and that the phoneme /l/ was reintroduced into Indo-Aryan as a result of contact with non-Indo-European inhabitants of the Subcontinent. [^liquid]

[^palatal-k]: See the summary of [Indo-European sound laws on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_sound_laws#Consonants), and also {% cite szemerenyi  --label section --locator 4.7.3 & 4.7.4 %}. In fact, the [centum-satem distinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centum_and_satem_languages) is precisely referencing this differentiation of \**ḱ* (the attested PIE form is \**ḱm̥tóm*, meaning "hundred", which leads to *centum* in Latin, a representative of the western branch, and *satem* in Avestan, a representative of the eastern branch).

[^liquid]: See {% cite szemerenyi  --label section --locator 4.4 %} and {% cite chatterji --locator 51  %}.

Assuming this is true, my first name may share etymological history with all of the following.

* The reconstructed Proto-Indo-Iranian [\**ĉarHád*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-Iranian/%C4%89arH%C3%A1d-) ("autumn; year"), which is the source of the following.
    * The Sanskrit noun *śarat* (शरत्, meaning "autumn; year").
        * It is loaned *tatsama* as শরত in Bengali, usually meaning "autumn."
        * In Hindustani, the Sanskrit is loaned *tatsama* as both शरद · <span dir="rtl">شرد</span> and शरत · <span dir="rtl">شرت</span>.[^sarat-sarad] There is also the *tadbhava* सरद · <span dir="rtl">سرد</span>. All of these usually mean "autumn."
    *  Persian سال ("year"), which in turn is the source of the Hindustani साल · سال ("year").
* The Persian adjective [سرد](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%B3%D8%B1%D8%AF) ("cold").
    * This in turn is the source of the Hindustani adjective सर्द · <span dir="rtl">سر</span> ("cold; indifferent") and the noun सर्दी · <span dir="rtl">سردی</span> ("coldness; winter; the common cold").
* The Lithuanian adjective [šáltas](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C5%A1altas) ("cold").[^lithuanian]
* The reconstructed Proto-Germanic noun [\**hehlǭ*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/hehl%C7%AD) ("hoarfrost").[^hehlo] This is the attested source of the following nouns, meaning "hoarfrost."
    * Old Norse [*héla*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/h%C3%A9la#Old_Norse), from which derives its [Icelandic homograph](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/h%C3%A9la#Icelandic).
    * Old English [*heolca*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heolca#Old_English).
* The reconstructed Proto-Germanic adjective [\**hlēwaz*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/hl%C4%93waz) ("lukewarm"). This is attested as the source of the following.
    * English [*lew*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lew#English) ("sunny; warm; tepid"), and possibly also the first part of  [*lukewarm*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lukewarm).
    * German [*lau*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lau#German) ("lukewarm").
    * Dutch [*lauw*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lauw#Dutch) ("lukewarm; cold; indifferent; relaxing").
    * Old Norse [hlær](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hl%C3%A6r) ("warm; mild").
* The Latin verb [*caleo*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caleo#Latin) ("to be warm or hot") and its many other derivative forms (eg, the noun [*calor*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/calor#Latin) ("warmth")). These lead to numerous descendents in Romance languages and in English.
    * French [*chaleur*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/chaleur#French) ("heat"), [*chaud*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/chaud#French) ("hot"), ...
    * Spanish [*calor*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/calor#Spanish) ("heat"),  [*caliente*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caliente#Spanish) ("hot"), ...
    * English *calorie*, ...

[^sarat-sarad]: Note that *śarat* is the form that the word takes phrase-finally; in other contexts, the word changes to *śarad* as dictated by the rules of Sanskrit *sandhi*. It is unclear to me why Hindustani should prefer the form *śarad* in this situation when usually its *tatsama* loans are the phrase-final forms, but this seems to be the case in this particular situation.

[^lithuanian]: Note that Lithuanian is a Balto-Slavic language. In particular, it is a satem language, which explains assibilation of the PIE \**k* into /ʃ/. Also, it is not Indo-Iranian, and we do indeed see faithful preservation of PIE \**l* as /l/.

[^hehlo]: Centum languages (such as Proto-Germanic) merged the palatovelar \**ḱ* with with the plain velar \**k*, and then phase 1 of [Grimm's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grimm%27s_law) came into affect, changing the voiceless velar stop  /k/ into the voiceless velar frivative /x/ (denoted with \**h*).

It's worth noting that there's a separate PIE root [\**gel*](https://lrc.la.utexas.edu/lex/master/0557) ("cold"), which is the source of Latin [*gelu*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gelu) and the English word *cold*.[^grimm]

[^grimm]: Phase 2 of [Grimm's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grimm%27s_law) proposes that the voiced velar stop \**g* in the PIE \**gel* became voiceless velar stop /k/ in Proto-Germanic, leading to the  Proto-Germanic [\**kaldaz*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/kaldaz) (the attested ancestor to English *cold* and German [*kalt*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kalt#German)).

## Literary references

Here are some occurrences of my first name in literature, organized by language. First up, we have Classical Sanskrit.

* The epic poem [*Buddhacarita* (बुद्धचरित)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhacarita) , by the 1st century Sanskrit poet [Aśvaghoṣa (अश्वघोष)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A%C5%9Bvagho%E1%B9%A3a), describes the life of the Buddha.     > khāt prasrute candramarīciśubhre dve vāridhāre śiśiroṣṇavīrye

* The poem [*R̥tusaṁhāra*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E1%B9%9Atusa%E1%B9%83h%C4%81ra) (ऋतुसंहार, "The Medley of Seasons"), by 4th-5th century Sanskrit poet [*Kālidāsa*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81lid%C4%81sa) (कालिदास),  describes natural phenomena characteristic of each of the six classical Indian seasons, and the reaction of lovers to these natural phenomena. Chapter 5 is  dedicated to the season *śiśira*, and here are some of the characteristics Kālidāsa describes.

        [^kraunca]: Scientific name: *Grus virgo*. Sanskrit name: *krauñca* (क्रौञ्च). Hindustani name: कूंज · کونج. See the [Wikipedia entry on the demoiselle crane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demoiselle_crane) for more information.

        [^sanskrit-translations]: I tried to translate from Sanskrit on my own, referencing {% cite apte  %} and various grammars available freely online. For the *R̥tusaṁhāra*, I then checked my translations against {% cite kale  %}.

        > वायवः सान्द्रतुषारशीतला  
        > vāyavaḥ sāndratuṣāraśītalā  
        > breezes chilled by dense frost,

        nights

        > तुषारसंघातनिपातशीतलाः शशाङ्कभाभिः शिशिरीकृतः पुनः  
        > tuṣārasaṃghātanipātaśītalāḥ śaśāṅkabhābhiḥ śiśirīkṛtāḥ punaḥ  
        > made cold by the falling of dew, and colder still by the light of the moon,

        > विपाण्डुतारागणचारुभूषणा  
        > vipāṇḍutārāgaṇacārubhūṣaṇā  
        > beautifully adorned by constellations as white as snow.   

    * Much of the chapter is an erotic (but heteronormative) description of lovers taking refuge from the long, cold winter nights in the warmth of each other's bodies. It also describes *śiśira* as...

        > प्रियजनरहितानां चितसंतापहेतुः  
        > priyajanarahitānāṃ citasaṃtāpahetuḥ  
        > the cause for the burning of the hearts of those without lovers.

        It goes to show that "cuffing season" and "winter cuddle buddies" are timeless, placeless phenomena.

* The [Viṣṇusahasranāma (विष्णुसहस्रनाम)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vishnu_sahasranama) is a [Vaishnavite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaishnavism) text listing a thousand names for [Viṣṇu (विष्णु)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vishnu). The nominative singular *śiśiraḥ*  occurs as name number 913 in this text.

    The original text is just a list of  names with no commentary. Later commentators over the centuries have provided their own unique commentaries for why each of the thousand names is used as a name for Viṣṇu. For the name *śiśiraḥ*, the gist of the commentaries that I've seen[^commentary] seems to be that Viṣṇu is seen as providing cooling relief to those that are burning (in worldly desires, in suffering, etc).

# Nickname {#nickname}

## Backstory

I was born in India a few weeks before my due date, and my dad was not in India at the time. It took him a couple of days to get to India after I was born. My parents had not settled on a name for me before I was born: they only settled on Shishir after my dad got to India. In the intervening days, my family wanted to have some name to refer to me by, and decided on Sunny.

The name has stuck ever since: almost everyone in my family invariably calls me Sunny, and some people have even been surprised to learn that my legal name is not actually Sunny.

I realized very soon after immigrating to the United States that Sunny was much easier for Americans to pronounce than Shishir, and I started telling my teachers, classmates, friends, students, etc, that they could call me Sunny. Most do.

## Pronunciation and orthography

The English pronunciation is just [ˈsʌ.ni].

The Hindustani pronunciation is usually the almost identical [səniː], though some Hindustani speakers over-faithfully preserve the English orthography in their pronunciation and [geminate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemination) the "n," saying [sənniː]. Accordingly, the Nagari orthography is usually सनी but occasionally सन्नी. In the Urdu alphabet, it is س‬ن‬ی‬.

In Japanese, I like writing サニ.
