This is a translation of a short piece of satire by famed author [Saadat Hasan Manto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saadat_Hasan_Manto) (1912-1955), published in a collection titled *Manto's Essays* {% cite manto_mazameen %}. The original piece can be found on [Rekhta](https://www.rekhta.org/articles/hindi-aur-urdu-saadat-hasan-manto-articles?lang=ur) in the Urdu alphabet, and a Nagari transliteration can be found [here]({% post_url 2018-12-12-हिंदी-और-उर्दू %}). All footnotes were added in translation in order to explain some relevant background information.

But first, a whirlwind synopsis of the historical and linguistic background for those who may need it...

**Historical and Linguistic Background.** After centuries of Persian influence via the Mughal courts, a language developed in South Asia. The core of the language (i.e., the grammatical structure and the bulk of the vocabulary) was decidedly Indo-Aryan, but loanwords from Persian (or from Arabic or Turkish via Persian) also abounded. The language was used both by court poets and by common folk of all creeds, especially in the Upper Gangetic Plains around Delhi. The orthography used to write a language is never a fundamental characteristic of the language itself, and certainly not in a society that is largely illiterate; in any case, the orthography that was usually used to write this language was a nativized variant of the Persian alphabet, adapted to meet some of the unique phonological needs of the language we're discussing.

While one can clearly identify this language in the historical record, assigning a name to this language turns out to be a complicated task. Historically, a variety of names were used: among them are Hindavi, Hindi, Hindustani, Bhasha, Rekhta, Khari Boli, and Urdu. Furthermore, some of these names sometimes referred to closely related but different languages (such as Braj Bhasha or Avadhi). A detailed discussion of the confusion surrounding naming can be found in {% cite chand --label chapter --locator III %}. In the 1800s, the name "Urdu" was in vogue.

Around this time, profound sectarianism began coursing through South Asia. The causes are unclear and manifold: fingers are often pointed at the influence of Christian missionaries and at the political structure of the British Raj, but many of the key figures of direct importance were native. In any case, one aspect of this sectarian pulse was a religious-nationalist movement related to the language we've been discussing that picked up steam after the [Indian Rebellion of 1857](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857). The goal of this movement was two-fold. The first goal was merely orthographic: the movement wanted for this language to be written in Nagari rather than the Urdu alphabet. The second goal was more substantial, and involved throwing out Persian loanwords and replacing them with words borrowed wholesale from Sanskrit (a language that had been dead in South Asia for many, many centuries, but that continued to hold religious significance for the Hindu population).

This movement began to push out propaganda describing Urdu as a language "for Muslims," and decided to use the name "Hindi" for the variant language they were championing, a language "for Hindus." (In anticipation of the following piece of satire, it is worth pointing out at this point that even the name "Hindi" itself is of Persian origin.) Adherents of this movement became the first of three camps in an intense ideological struggle; let us call this "Camp Hindi."

"Camp Urdu" presented an opposite reactionary vision. Adherents of this camp would attempt to eliminate all Indo-Aryan vocabulary from their writing and replace them with Persian loanwords, to the point that one could find "whole sentences in which the only Indian thing is the grammar, and with nothing but Persian words from beginning to end" {% cite grierson --locator 45 in vol. 9 pt. 1 %}.

And then there was "Camp Hindustani," the center of the spectrum spanned by Camp Hindi on one end and Camp Urdu on the other. They emphasized the language that was actually used by the masses, distinctly Indo-Aryan but with a healthy sprinkling of Persian loanwords. With the other two camps polarizing the usage of the names "Hindi" and "Urdu," some in Camp Hindustani tried to popularize the name "Hindustani" to refer to this common core (but this was not universal: some people who used the terms "Hindi" or "Urdu," including Manto himself, would likely still be placed ideologically in Camp Hindustani). Some in Camp Hindustani also proposed a compromise orthographic system: both Nagari and the Urdu alphabet would be recognized scripts for writing this language, and students would be educated in both orthographic systems.

Needless to say, the above discussion simplifies a great deal, and the interested reader will find numerous and voluminous tomes written about the Hindi-Urdu fissure (e.g., {% cite king %}, {% cite chand %}, {% cite grierson --label "" --locator vol. 9 pt. 1 %}, among many others). In any case, by the 1940s, independence was looming just around the corner and questions about the "national language" of the soon-to-be-independent state(s) had brought this debate to the political forefront. The arguments reached fever pitch.

And then Partition befell the Subcontinent. The extremes won out politically: the vision of Camp Hindi became enshrined as one of the official languages of the Government of India (alongside English), and the vision of Camp Urdu became enshrined as one of the official languages of Pakistan (again, alongside English).

Of course, none of this intellectual and political brouhaha has had much bearing on the language as it used by the masses. Even today, speakers in India can largely understand speakers in Pakistan, and conversely, except when the diction becomes highly Sanskritized or highly Persianized. Perhaps the only significant impact of the Hindi-Urdu debates is that, since literacy rose substantially only after independence, most people on both sides of the border cannot read written work produced on the other side until it is transliterated.

The hysterical tenor and simultaneous pointlessness of the Hindi-Urdu debates is the object of satire in the following.

The Hindi-Urdu squabble has been going on for an age. Maulvi[^maulvi] Abdul Haq[^abdulhaq], Dr. Tara Chand[^tarachand], and Mahatma Gandhi[^gandhi] all seem to understand this squabble, but it's still completely beyond me. I've tried, but I just can't figure it out. Why are Hindus wasting their time talking about the Right of Hindi[^haq]? Why are Muslims getting all worked up about the preservation of Urdu? Languages aren't made, they simply arise; and no human efforts can eradicate them. I wanted to write something about this fresh and heated topic, and the result was the following dialogue.

[^maulvi]: [*Maulvi*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mawlawi_(Islamic_title)) is a title used for Muslim religious scholars.

[^abdulhaq]: [Abdul Haq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdul_Haq_(Urdu_scholar)) (1870-1961) was an avid proponent of Urdu and a very prominent figure in the Hindi-Urdu debates around the time of partition. His championship of Urdu donned him the epithet *Baba-e-Urdu*, meaning "Father of Urdu."

[^tarachand]: Tara Chand (1888-1973) was a historian. He describes his views about the Hindi-Urdu debates in a small book titled *The Problem of Hindustani* {% cite chand %}.

[^gandhi]: [Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi) (1869-1948) needs no introduction. As far as the Hindi-Urdu debates go, he can be placed fairly solidly in "Camp Hindustani" as described in the historical background above: he encouraged the use of the term "Hindustani" to refer to the language as it was spoken by laypeople, and wanted both orthographies to be accepted ways of writing the language.

[^haq]: "Camp Hindi" used the phrase "the Right of Hindi" (हिंदी का हक · <span dir="rtl">ہندی کا حق</span>, or sometimes the Sanskritized variant हिंदी का अधिकार · <span dir="rtl">ہندی کا ادھکار</span>) frequently during the Hindi-Urdu debates, the idea being that their Sanskritized vision of "Hindi" had a *right* to be the national language of India, and that Hindus had to demand this right. It continues to be used occasionally today (for instance, in demands that Hindi be recognized as an [Official Language of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations), or in deploring the usage of English or Hinglish by the Indian media). I do not have access to enough data to pinpoint the precise origins of the phrase.   

Munshi[^munshi] Narayan Prashad: Iqbal Sahab[^sahab], are you going to drink this club soda?

Mirza[^mirza] Mohammad Iqbal: Yes.

[^munshi]: *[Munshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munshi)* is a title used for scholars, scribes, or translators.
[^sahab]: *[Sahab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahib)* is a title used broadly to convey respect.
[^mirza]: *[Mirza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirza)* is a title used for royal princes, high noblemen, military commanders, or scholars.

Munshi: Why don't you drink lemon soda?

Iqbal: No reason, I just like club soda. It's what everyone in my family drinks.

Munshi: So you hate lemon soda.

Iqbal: No... Why would I hate it, Munshi Narayan Prashad? It's just that everyone in my family drinks club soda, so I've gotten used to it. It's not a big deal, and actually I think that lemon soda is more enjoyable than club soda.

Munshi: That's why I'm surprised. Why would you prefer an acrid drink over a sweet one? Lemon soda is not only sweet, it also smells nice. Don't you think?

Iqbal: You're absolutely right. But...

Munshi: But what?

Iqbal: Nothing... I was just about to say that I'm going to have club soda anyway.

Munshi: Senseless! If someone was listening, they'd think I was forcing you to drink poison... Come on, is there even any difference between lemon soda and club soda? The same factory makes both bottles. The same machine fills them both up. And besides, if you take the sweetness and the fragrance out of lemon soda, what would you be left with?

Iqbal: Club soda... Acrid water...

Munshi: So what's the problem with drinking lemon soda?

Iqbal: There's no problem.

Munshi: Then go on, drink some.

Iqbal: What will you[^you] drink?

[^you]: Hindustani has three second person pronouns which differ in the formality they convey (i.e., it has a three-way [T-V distinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction)). The dialogue begins with both participants speaking at the highest level of formality. At this point, Mirza Mohammad Iqbal drops down to the middle level of formality. This lapse in social decorum is perhaps indicative of Iqbal's building irritation.

Munshi: I'll order another bottle.

Iqbal: Why would you order another bottle? What's wrong with drinking my club soda?

Munshi: Th... There's no problem.

Iqbal: Then go on, drink some club soda.

Munshi: What will you[^you2] drink?

[^you2]: At this point, Munshi Narayan Prashad also lapses to the middle level of formality.

Iqbal: I'll... I'll order another bottle.

Munshi: Why will you order another bottle? What's the problem with drinking lemon soda?

Iqbal: There's... no... problem... And what's the problem with drinking club soda?

Munshi: There's... no... problem.

Iqbal: It's just that club soda is a little better.

Munshi: Lemon — I think lemon soda is a little better.

Iqbal: That may be... But I grew up hearing that club soda is better.

Munshi: Now what... I also grew up hearing that lemon soda is better.

Iqbal: What's *your*[^you3] opinion?

[^you3]: Here Mirza Mohammad Iqbal switches back to using the most formal second person pronouns. He maintains this distance for the remainder of the dialogue.

Munshi: What's *your*[^you4] opinion?

[^you4]: Here Munshi Narayan Prashad also switches back to using the most formal second person pronouns. He also maintains this distance for the remainder of the dialogue.

Iqbal: My opinion... My opinion... I think that... But why don't you tell me what you think?

Munshi: My opinion... My opinion... I think that... But why should I be the first one to say what I think?

Iqbal: No one's going to say what they think this way... Let's just cover up our glasses so that we can take our time to settle this.

Munshi: We can't do that... The bottles have already been opened. Now we'll have to drink them. Let's decide quickly. We don't want them to go flat... The entire point of soda is the carbonation.

Iqbal: I agree... And you also accepted that there's no difference between lemon soda and club soda.

Munshi: When did I say that there's no difference? There's a lot of difference... A world of difference. One is sweet and sour and smells nice. That's three things it has more of than club soda. Club soda is just carbonation, and the carbonation is so strong that you feel it in your nose. Lemon soda is just more enjoyable. You drink a bottle, and you feel good for hours afterwards. Only invalids drink club soda... Besides, you just accepted that lemon soda is more enjoyable than club soda.

Iqbal: Okay. But I didn't say that it's *better* than club soda. Enjoyable doesn't mean that it's good for you. Pickles[^pickle] are enjoyable but you surely know how bad they are for you. Just because a thing is sour and smells nice doesn't make it obviously a good thing. Just ask any doctor and he'll tell you that lemon soda is bad for your stomach.

Munshi: Look, I think the only way to settle this is to mix the two of them together.

Iqbal: Sounds good to me.

Munshi: Pour half of the club soda into this empty glass.

Iqbal: Why don't you put half of the lemon soda in the glass... I'll put the club soda in afterwards.

Munshi: That doesn't make any sense. Why don't you pour the club soda in first?

Iqbal: I want club soda mixed with lemon soda.

Munshi: And I want lemon soda mixed with club soda.


[^pickle]: The reference is to [South Asian pickles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asian_pickles): fruits and vegetables pickled in oil or brine with a lot of spices. They are usually spicy, sour, and very fragrant. I am not aware of health concerns associated to these pickles, but maybe rumors of such concerns were prevalent at the time of this writing.
