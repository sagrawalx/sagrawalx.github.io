<div class="introduction">
Here is an unabashedly modified version of the Prajñāpāramitāhṛdayasūtra (known in English as the "Heart Sutra"). It's shorter than the short version. This was mostly an exercise for me to learn some Sanskrit. 

I conferred the Sanskrit versions on [Bibliotheca Polygotta](https://www2.hf.uio.no/polyglotta/index.php?page=fulltext&view=fulltext&vid=836&cid=638829&mid=1476628), [Wikisource](https://sa.wikisource.org/wiki/%E0%A4%AA%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B0%E0%A4%9C%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%9E%E0%A4%BE%E0%A4%AA%E0%A4%BE%E0%A4%B0%E0%A4%AE%E0%A4%BF%E0%A4%A4%E0%A4%BE%E0%A4%B9%E0%A5%83%E0%A4%A6%E0%A4%AF) (which is the same as the version in the [Māhāyāna Sūtra Saṃgraha, Part I](https://archive.org/details/in.ernet.dli.2015.405222/), edited by P. L. Vaidya and published in 1961 by the Mithila Institute), and in Edward Conze's [Buddhist Wisdom Books](https://archive.org/details/buddhistwisdombo00harp/) (published 1958 by Harper Torchbooks). Besides Conze's translation, I also consulted [Śraddhāpa's translation](https://www.sraddhapa.com/hrdaya). I then excised and modified things in order to make the whole feel concise and coherent to me.

Here are a few words about some (but not all) of the changes I made. 

* I removed the line which reads *pancaskandhāṃs tāṃś ca svabhāvaśūnyān paśyati sma*, since this repeats something that follows later. I also removed a later enumeration of the five skandhas. 

* The longer version of the Heart Sutra tells a story in which the Buddha, Avalokiteshvara, and Śāriputra are all characters. Both of the short versions remove the Buddha from the story. They also essentially remove Śāriputra from the story, but Avalokiteshvara's vocative addresses to Śariputra are retained. This seems silly to me, so I excised them. 

* One part reads *na cakṣūrdhātur yāvan na manovijñānadhatuḥ*. The long ū is probably a typo. In any case, in isolation, this seems to be a condensed list to the eighteen dhātus (which starts with *cakṣurdhatu*, ie, the eye sense-dhātu, and ends *manovijñānadhatu*, the mind consciousness-dhātu). But the first twelve elements of this list (the sense-dhātus and the sense-object-dhātus) have already been listed in the sutra. I thus replaced this phrase with *na cakṣūrvijñanadhātur yāvan na manovijñānadhatuḥ*, so that what we have is a condensed list of just the six consciousness-dhātus. 

* The originals also contain an ebullient introduction to the mantra of the heart sutra. I excised some of this ebullience, because it seemed excessive to me. 
</div>

[^dhatu]: There are eighteen elements (dhātu) in all knowable objects (jñeya). There are six sense-organ elements (indriya-dhātu) which process information, six sense-object elements (viṣaya-dhātu) which form the field of what the sense-organ elements process, and finally six consciousness elements (vijñāna-dhātu) which bring the objects into cognition. These are as follows: 

    * Visual: eye (cakṣur-dhātu), form (rūpa-dhātu), and visual consciosness (cakṣur-vijñāna-dhatu). 
    * Auditory: ear (śrota-dhātu), sound (śabda-dhātu), and auditory consciousness (śrota-vijñāna-dhatu)
    * Olfactory: nose (ghrāna-dhātu), smell (gandha-dhātu), and olfactory consciousness (ghrāna-vijñāna-dhatu)
    * Gustatory: tongue (jihvā-dhātu), taste (rasa-dhātu), and gustatory consciousness (jihvā-vijñāna-dhatu)
    * Tactile: body (kāya-dhātu), tactile objects (spraṣṭavya-dhātu), and tacticle consciousness (kaya-vijñāna-dhatu)
    * Mental: mind (mano-dhātu), mental object (dharma-dhātu), and mental consciousness (mano-vijñāna-dhatu). 
    
    The assertion being made here is that all of these are empty.

[^mantra]: [Here](https://www2.kenyon.edu/Depts/Religion/Fac/Adler/Reln260/Heartmantra.htm) is a grammatical analysis of this mantra by Richard Hayes, but I don't think this analysis is completely sound. The issue is that the vocative singular of the i-stem noun bodhi- should be bodhe (whether it is treated as masculine or feminine). The declension of this stem closest to "bodhi" is the nominative singular bodhiḥ, but external sandhi of ḥ + s does not erase the ḥ. The point is, I think the "bodhi" in this mantra is just an undeclined stem. This makes the phrase grammatically incoherent as a whole, but it is a mantra so it does not need to be grammatically coherent. In fact, if the mantra is to be interpretted in part as a "fuck you" to brahminical orthodoxy (as Richard Hayes suggests on this page), perhaps this is better expressed by flouting the grammatical rules of the brahminical orthodoxy's sacred language.
