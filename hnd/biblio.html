Hindustani: An Annotated Bibliography

Here are numerous references that deal with the history of Hindustani and the Hindi-Urdu debates, ordered alphabetically by the last name of the author. Included below each one are brief summaries and/or relevant excerpts. Please let me know if you find any broken links or other problems of any kind.

  • Grierson, George Abraham (1909). Imperial Gazetteer of India, Volume 1, Chapter VII. Oxford: Clarendon Press. [Link]
    • This chapter very broadly describes the state of Indian philology in the early 1900s. One observation the author makes is that language names (as distinct from dialect names) are a chiefly European construction. He classifies Hindustani as a dialect of Western Hindi (alongside the other dialects of Haryanvi, Braj, Kanauji, and Bundeli). Urdu and Hindi are called “forms” of Hindustani. The author points out that the importance of this dialect arises not from its population of native speakers (who are outnumbered by those of Bengali), but from its prominent role in the subcontinental marketplace. The author asserts that the “Persian element was first introduced in excess” by Hindu officials in the Mughal court. The author also attributes the origins of Urdu prose, and of the Sanskritized Hindi that ejects words of Perso-Arabic origins, to Fort William College.

  • Lahiri, Madhumati (2015). ‘An Idiom for India.’’ Interventions. [DOI]
    • This article describes the evolution of the “Hindustani project,” starting from the early days of British presence in the subcontinent, through the present. According to the author, the project begins with linguist Scottish linguist John Gilchrist’s distrust for grandiloquent native teachers and their penchant for obscure loans from Sanskrit, Persian, and Arabic (the author notes the racist tendencies of Gilchrist, but does not attribute blame for the Hindi-Urdu fissure to the British). The next phase is dominated by the political aspirations of the Indian National Congress and the Progressive Writers’ Movement. This includes Nehru’s vision of Basic Hindustani, the PWM’s vision of “a common language (Hindustani) and a common script (Indi-Roman) for all of India,” and Premchand’s Hindustani zubaan. In the postcolonial period, the author argues that, despite official “erasure” of Hindustani on the political front, the Hindustani project survives largely through Bollywood cinema and its market aspirations. The author argues that the language of Bollywood is an “imaginary,” a language which does not exist anywhere but which allows access to the largest possible audience. Quotes by Khwaja Ahmad Abbas play a prominent role in this section of the paper.

  • Rushdie, Salman (2005). ‘The Art of Fiction No. 186.’ Interview with Jack Livings. Paris Review. [Link]

    INTERVIEWER: Bombay has many languages. What is your mother tongue?

    RUSHDIE: Urdu. Urdu is literally my mother’s tongue. It’s my father’s tongue, too. But in northern India one also spoke Hindi. Actually, what we spoke was neither of them, or rather more like both. I mean, what people in northern India actually speak is not a real language. It’s a colloquial mixture of Hindi and Urdu called Hindustani. It isn’t written. It’s the language of Bollywood movies. And some mixture of Hindustani and English is what we spoke at home.
