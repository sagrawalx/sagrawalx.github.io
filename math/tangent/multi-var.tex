\chapter{Multivariable derivatives} \label{multivar}

In this chapter, we will study derivatives of functions $S \to \R^n$ where $S$ is a subset of $\R^m$. We dealt with the case $m = n = 1$ in the previous chapter, starting with a very geometric idea of tangent lines to graphs. For large values of $m$ and $n$, graphs become hard to visualize and it is not so clear what ``tangent'' should mean. But there is at least one higher dimensional case when we can use some geometry to formalize what ``tangent'' should mean, which is the case $m = 2$ and $n = 1$. The graph of such a function is a surface in $\R^3$, and its ``tangent'' at a point should be a plane. We'll begin by analyzing a particular example of this situation to build up some geometric intuition, and then proceed with the abstract discussion of multivariable derivatives for general $m$ and $n$. 

\section{An extended example}

Let us begin with an example. Our analysis of this example will presage many of the concepts we will discuss later in the chapter. 
The example we'll consider is the function $f : \R^2 \to \R$ defined by
\[ f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 + x_2^2. \]

\subsection{Describing the graph of $f$}

First off, let's try to get a solid understanding of the graph of $f$. The graph $\Gamma$ is a subset of $\R^2 \times \R = \R^3$ defined by
\[ \Gamma = \{ (x_1, x_2, y) : y = f(x_1, x_2) \} = \{ (x_1, x_2, y) : y = x_1^2  + x_2^2 \}. \]
To visualize $\Gamma$, we will repeatedly slice $\Gamma$ along various planes. 

If we consider the slice obtained by setting $x_1 = 0$, we find a parabola $y = x_2^2$. Similarly, if we consider the slice obtained by setting $x_1 = 1$, we obtain the parabola $y = 1 + x_2^2$. Fixing $x_1 = 5$, we obtain the parabola $y = 25 + x^2$. In fact, we can see that no matter what value of $x_1$ we fix, the resulting slice is always a parabola, just translated up from the origin by differing amounts (that amount being $x_1^2$). 

It's also worth considering the ``level sets,'' which are the slices of $\Gamma$ obtained by fixing various values of $y$. When $y = 0$, there is just a single point $x_1 = x_2 = 0$. Fixing $y = 1$, the slice is given by $1 = x_1^2 +  x_2^2$, which is a circle of radius 1. Fixing $y = 17$, the slice is $17 = x_1^2 + x_2^2$, which is circle of radius $\sqrt{17}$. In fact, all of the level sets are circles. 

Putting these slices together, we see that the graph $\Gamma$ is a ``big parabolic bowl.''

\subsection{Tangent plane at $a = (2,1)$}

Now that we understand what the graph of $f$ looks like, let's try to figure out what the ``tangent plane'' $T$ at a point $a = (2,1)$ will look like. Of course, it's a plane inside $\R^3$ that passes through the point $(2, 1, f(2,1)) = (2, 1, 5)$. To specify which plane it actually is, we start by describing some lines that lie on this plane. 

Consider the $x_2 = 1$ slice. We know that the graph of $\Gamma$ along this slice is the parabola $y = x_1^2 + 1$. So, slicing the tangent plane $T$ along $x_2 = 1$ should yield the tangent line to this function of one variable, which we know how to compute from \cref{single-var}. The slope of this tangent line is
\[ \left.\frac{d f(x_1, 1)}{d x_1}\right|_{x_1 = 2} = 4. \]
This quantity is called the \emph{first partial derivative of $f$}, and will be denoted $\partial_1 f(a)$ later.  Thus the tangent line is the line parametrized by
\[ h_1 \mapsto (2,1,5) + h_1 \cdot (1, 0, 4). \]
Similarly, we can consider the $x_1 = 2$ slice. We know that the graph $\Gamma$ along this slice is the parabola $y = 4 + x_2^2$.The slope of the corresponding tangent line is
\[ \partial_2 f(a) = \left.\frac{d f(2, x_2)}{dx_2}\right|_{x_2 = 1} = 2. \]
Thus this tangent line is the line parametrized by
\[ h_2 \mapsto (2,1,5) + h_2 \cdot (0,1,2). \]

These two lines must uniquely determine the entire plane $T$. One way of describing $T$ that immediately falls out of the parametrizations we've found of the tangent lines is the plane parametrized by
\[ \begin{bmatrix} h_1 \\ h_2 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} h_1 \\ h_2 \\ 4h_1 + 2h_2 \end{bmatrix}. \]
The important part of this expression is the bottom entry on the far right, the $4h_1 + 2h_2$. To see why, consider the function $\R^2 \to \R$ given by $(h_1, h_2)  \mapsto 4h_1 + 2h_2$. This is what we will call the \emph{(total) derivative} of $f$ and denote by $df_a$. Notice that $df_a$ is a linear map $\R^2 \to \R$. Moreover, its graph is a plane passing through the origin that is parallel to $T$. In other words, if we take the graph of $df_a$ and translate it over to the point $(2,1,5)$, we obtain exactly the tangent plane $T$. Speaking more loosely, the derivative $df_a : \R^2 \to \R$ records all of the ``slope-y information'' about the tangent plane $T$. 

Notice moreover that the standard matrix representation $[df_a]$ of $df_a$ is \begin{equation} \label{jacobian-matrix-example} [df_a] = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \partial_1 f(a) & \partial_2 f(a) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation}

\subsection{$df_a$ as an approximation}

Consider the function $\epsilon : \R^2 \to \R$ defined by
\[ v \mapsto |f(a+v)-f(a)-df_a(v)|.  \]
If $v = (h_1, h_2)$, we have
\[ \epsilon(v) = \epsilon\left( \begin{bmatrix} h_1 \\ h_2 \end{bmatrix} \right) \mapsto |(2+h_1)^2 + (1+h_2)^2 - 5 - 4h_1 - 2h_2| = |h_1^2 + h_2^2| = \left|\begin{bmatrix} h_1 \\ h_2 \end{bmatrix} \right|^2 = |v|^2. \]
Notice that $\epsilon$ gets small rapidly as $v \to 0$. More precisely, notice that
\[ \lim_{v \to 0} \frac{\epsilon(v)}{|v|} = \lim_{v \to 0} |v| = 0, \]
so $\epsilon = o(|v|)$ as $v \to 0$. 

Since $\epsilon$ gets small rapidly, the function $v \mapsto f(a) + df_a(v)$ is a good approximation of the function $v \mapsto f(a+v)$ for small values of $v$. Said differently, letting $x = a + v$, the function $x \mapsto f(a) + df_a(x-a)$ is a good approximation of $f$ near $a$. 

\subsection{Overview}

In what follows, we will turn the above example on its head and \emph{define} the derivative using approximations. In other words, the derivative $df_a$ will be defined to be a linear function which yields a good approximation of $f$ near a point $a$. Then we will prove in \cref{jacobian-matrix} that the partial derivatives can be used to describe $df_a$, as we noted in \cref{jacobian-matrix-example} above. The reason for doing this is that there are some bizarre functions where partial derivatives make sense, but tangent planes do not (cf. \cref{partials-dont-imply-differentiable} below).

As you may be able to tell, we'll be using linear algebra heavily in this chapter. If you need a brief refresher, check out \cref{linear-algebra}. We'll also need some facts about the operator norm; you may not have seen this before, and can refer to \cref{operator-norm} as needed. 

\section{Definition of the derivative}

Throughout, $S$ will denote a subset of $\R^m$. 

\begin{definition}
	A function $f : S \to \R^n$ is \emph{differentiable at} an interior point $a \in S$ if there exists a linear map $\ell : \R^m \to \R^n$ such that
	\[ |f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v)| = o(|v|) \text{ as } v \to 0. \]
\end{definition}

It turns out that there can exist at most one linear map that has the above property. 

\begin{lemma}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R^m$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$. If $\ell$ and $\ell'$ are both linear maps $\R^m \to \R^n$ such that
	\[ |f(a+v) - f(a) - \ell(v)| = o(|v|) \text { and } |f(a+v) - f(a) - \ell'(v)| = o(|v|) \text{ as } v \to 0, \]
	then $\ell = \ell'$. 
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
	Let $\phi = \ell' - \ell$. Then $\phi$ is also a linear map $\R^m \to \R^n$. Moreover, observe that
	\[ \begin{aligned} |\phi(v)| &= |\ell'(v) - \ell(v)| \\
	&= |(f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v))-(f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell'(v)| \\
	&\leq |f(a+h)-f(a)-\ell(v)| + |f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell'(v)|. \end{aligned} \]
	Since both $|f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v)|$ and $|f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell'(v)|$ are $o(|v|)$, \cref{less-than-small-is-small,little-o-vector-space} imply that $|\phi(v)| = o(|v|)$ also. Then \cref{linear-and-small} implies that $\phi = 0$. 
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$. Then the \emph{total derivative}, or just \emph{derivative}, of $f$ at $a$, denoted $df_a$, is the unique linear map $\R^m \to \R^n$ satisfying
	\[ |f(a+v)-f(a)-df_a(v)| = o(|v|) \text{ as } v \to 0.  \]
\end{definition}

Few examples can be computed directly from the definition. 

\begin{exercise} \label{derivative-of-linear}
	Suppose $\ell : \R^m \to \R^n$ is a linear map. Prove that $d\ell_a = \ell$ for all $a \in \R^m$.  
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise} \label{derivative-multiplication}
	Let $f : \R^2 \to \R$ be the map $f(x_1,x_2) = x_1x_2$. Use the definition of the derivative to prove that
	\[ df_{a}(h_1, h_2) = a_2h_1 + a_1 h_2 \]
	for all $a = (a_1, a_2) \in \R^2$. 
\end{exercise}

We will develop a bit more theory in order to compute derivatives more easily. Meanwhile, we can prove the following directly from the definition. 

\begin{exercise} \label{differentiable-implies-continuous}
	If $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$, show that $f$ must be continuous at $a$. 
\end{exercise}

\section{Computing derivatives}

\subsection{Sum and scalar multiples rule}

\begin{exercise}[Sum rule] \label{sum-rule}
	Prove that, if $f, g : S \to \R^n$ are both differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$, then $f + g$ is also differentiable at $a$ and \[ d(f+g)_a = df_a + dg_a. \]
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Scalar multiples rule] \label{scalar-multiples-rule}
	Prove that, if $c$ is a constant and $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$, then $cf$ is also differentiable at $a$ and \[ d(cf)_a = c \cdot df_a. \]
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Chain rule}

The proof of the following is essentially the same as the second proof of the single variable chain rule given in \cref{chain-rule-single-second-proof}. Since this proof is basically a repeat, some details are omitted; you are asked to fill in the details in \cref{chain-rule-details} below. 

\begin{theorem}[Chain rule] \label{chain-rule}
	Suppose that $S$ and $T$ are subsets of $\R^m$ and $\R^n$, respectively, that $f : S \to T$ is differentiable at an interior point $a$, that $f(a)$ is an interior point of $T$, and that $g : T \to \R^p$ is differentiable at $f(a)$. Then the composite $g \circ f : S \to \R^p$ is also differentiable at $a$, and 
	\[ d(g \circ f)_a = dg_{f(a)} \circ df_a. \]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
	Define the ``error in approximation'' functions
	\[ \begin{aligned} 
	r(v) &= f(a+v)-f(a) - df_a(v) \\ 
	s(w) &= g(f(a)+w)-g(f(a))-dg_{f(a)}(w) 
	\end{aligned} \]
	and then observe that
	\begin{equation} \label{rewrite-error} g(f(a+v)) - g(f(a)) - dg_{f(a)}(df_a(v)) = dg_{f(a)}(r(v)) + s(df_a(v)+r(v)). \end{equation}
	This means that 
	\[ |g(f(a+v)) - g(f(a)) - dg_{f(a)}(df_a(v))| \leq |dg_{f(a)}(r(v))| + |s(df_a(h)+r(v))|. \]
	Since $dg_{f(a)}$ is linear and $|r(v)| = o(|v|)$, \cref{linear-of-small-is-small} guarantees that $|dg_{f(a)}(r(v))| = o(|h|)$ also. Thus it is sufficient to prove that $|s(df_a(v) + r(v))| = o(|v|)$. To do this, define $\eta(w) = |s(w)|/|w|$ and then notice that 
	\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{|s(df_a(v)+r(v))|}{|v|} &= \eta(df_a(v)+r(v)) \cdot \frac{|df_a(v) + r(v)|}{|v|} \\
	&\leq \eta(df_a(v) + r(v)) \left( \|df_a\| + \frac{|r(v)|}{|v|} \right). \end{aligned} \]
	We now take the limit as $h \to 0$. Using the facts that $\eta \circ (df_a + r)$ is continuous at 0 and that $|r(v)| = o(|v|)$, we obtain the result. 
\end{proof}

\begin{exercise} \label{chain-rule-details}
	\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
		\item Prove \cref{rewrite-error}. 
		\item If you haven't done it already, do \cref{linear-of-small-is-small}.
		\item Prove that $\eta \circ (df_a + r)$ is continuous at 0. 
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Differentiability by components}

Here we show that it's ``enough'' to consider the case $n = 1$. 

\begin{definition}
	For $j = 1, \dotsc, n$, let $\pi_j : \R^n \to \R$ denote the $j$th \emph{projection map}
	\[ \pi_j(x_1, \dotsc, x_n) = x_j. \]
	Then, for any function $f : S \to \R^n$, we define the $j$th \emph{component function} $f_j : S \to \R$ to be the composite $\pi_j \circ f$. 
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma} \label{differentiable-by-components}
	A function $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$ if and only if the component function $f_j : S \to \R$ is differentiable at $a$ for all $j = 1, \dotsc n$. Moreover,
	\begin{equation} \label{derivative-of-components} df_a(v) = \begin{bmatrix} df_{1,a}(v) \\ \vdots \\ df_{n,a}(v) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
	If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then the composite $f_j = \pi_j \circ f$ is also differentiable at $a$ by the chain rule \ref{chain-rule}. Moreover, since $\pi_j$ is linear, we know that $d\pi_{j,f(a)} = \pi_j$ by \cref{derivative-of-linear}. Thus, by the chain rule, 
	\[ df_{j,a}(v) = (d\pi_{j,f(a)} \circ df_a)(v) = \pi_j(df_a(v)) \]
	for all $j$, which is precisely \cref{derivative-of-components}. 
	For the converse, we turn \cref{derivative-of-components} on its head. Suppose $f_j$ is differentiable at $a$ for all $j$, and let $\ell : \R^m \to \R^n$ be the linear map 
	\[ \ell(v) = \begin{bmatrix} df_{1,a}(v) \\ \vdots \\ df_{n,a}(v) \end{bmatrix}.  \]
	We now want to show that $|f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v)| = o(|v|)$ as $v \to 0$. Observe that the component functions of $v \mapsto f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v)$ are given by
	\[ v \mapsto \pi_j \left( f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v) \right) = f_j(a+v)-f_j(a)-df_{j,a}(v), \]
	and we know that $|f_j(a+v)-f_j(a)-df_{j,a}(v)| = o(|v|)$. By \cref{components-are-small-implies-small} below applied with the function $r(v) = f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(v)$, we conclude that $|f(a+v)-f(a)-\ell(h)| = o(|v|)$. 
\end{proof}

\begin{exercise} \label{components-are-small-implies-small}
	\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
		\item For any vector $w \in \R^n$, show that \[ \displaystyle |w| \leq \sqrt{n} \max_j |\pi_j(w)|. \]
		\item Show that, if $S$ is a neighborhood of 0 in $\R^m$ and $r :  S  \to \R^n$ is a function such that $|r_j(v)| = o(|v|)$ as $v \to 0$ for all $j = 1, \dotsc, n$, then $|r(v)| = o(|v|)$ also. \textit{Hint.} Use part (a). 
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Partial derivatives}

Here we focus on the case $n = 1$. In other words, we consider functions whose input is $m$ real numbers, and whose output is just a single number. 

\begin{definition}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R$ is a function and $a \in S$ is an interior point. The $i$th \emph{partial derivative} of $f$ at $a$, denoted \[ \partial_i f(a) \text{ or } \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(a) \] is defined to be the limit
	\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+he_i) - f(a)}{h}. \]
\end{definition}

You may remember from multivariable calculus that partial derivatives behave a great deal like single variable derivatives. The reason for this is the following important remark, which reinterprets the partial derivative as a single variable derivative. 

\begin{remark} \label{partial-to-single}
	Consider the function $\xi_i : \R \to \R^m$ given by $\xi_i(h) = a + he_i$. Then 0 is an interior point of $T = \xi_i^{-1}(S) \subseteq \R$, and $f \circ \xi_i$ is a single variable function $T \to \R$. Moreover, we have
	\[ \partial_i f(a) = (f \circ \xi_i)'(0). \]
\end{remark}

\begin{example} \label{computing-partials-example}
	Consider the function $f : \R^2 \to \R$ given by $f(x_1, x_2) = \sin(x_1x_2^2)$. Let $a = (a_1, a_2) \in \R^2$ and define $\xi_1$ as in \cref{partial-to-single}. Then
	\[ (f \circ \xi_1)(h) = f(a_1 + h, a_2) = \sin((a_1+h)a_2^2).  \]
	Then
	\[ \begin{aligned} \partial_1 f(a) &= (f \circ \xi_1)'(0) \\
	&= \left.\frac{d}{dh} \left(\sin((a_1+h)a_2^2) \right) \right|_{h = 0} \\
	&= \left. a_2^2\cos((a_1+h)a_2^2)\right|_{h = 0} \\
	&= a_2^2\cos(a_1a_2^2). \end{aligned}  \]
\end{example}

\begin{exercise}
	With notation as in \cref{computing-partials-example}, compute $\partial_2 f(a)$. 
\end{exercise}

We can also use \cref{partial-to-single} to prove some partial derivative analogs of results we proved in \cref{single-var}.

\begin{exercise}[Product rule for partial derivatives] \label{product-rule-partials}
	Suppose $f, g : S \to \R$ are functions and $a \in S$ is an interior point such that $\partial_i f(a)$ and $\partial_i g(a)$ both exist. Prove that $\partial_i (fg)(a)$ also exists and that
	\[ \partial_i (fg)(a) = \partial_i(f)(a) g(a) + f(a) \partial_i g(a). \]
	\textit{Hint}. Use \cref{partial-to-single} and the statement of the single variable product rule \ref{product-cn-singlevar}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[Interior extremum theorem] \label{interior-extremum-multivar}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R$ is a function and an interior point $a \in S$ is a local extremum of $f$. Show that, if $\partial_i f(a)$ exists, then $\partial_i f(a) = 0$. \textit{Hint}. Use \cref{partial-to-single} and the single variable interior extremum theorem \ref{interior-extremum}. 
\end{exercise}

We have the same caveat to the interior extremum theorem that we had in the single variable case (cf. \cref{interior-extremum-converse-false}), except that there are even more ways for the converse to fail. 

\begin{example}
	Consider the function $f : \R^2 \to \R$ given by \[ f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 - x_2^2. \]
	Let $\xi_i(h) = 0 + h e_i$ for $i = 1, 2$. Then $f \circ \xi_1$ has a minimum at 0, so $(f \circ \xi_1)'(0) = \partial_1 f(0) = 0$. Similarly, $f \circ \xi_2$ has a maximum at 0, so $(f \circ \xi_2)'(0) = \partial_2 f(0) = 0$ also. But clearly these two facts together mean that 0 cannot be a local extremum of $f$.

The main reason we care about partial derivatives is that they help us compute derivatives. Note that we will generalize this result in \cref{jacobian-matrix} below. For more about the notation $[df_a]$, see \cref{matrix-representations}.

\begin{lemma} \label{differentiability-implies-partials}
	If $f : S \to \R$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$, then the $i$th partial derivative $\partial_i f(a)$ exists for all $i = 1, \dotsc m$, and the standard matrix representation $[df_a]$ of the linear map $df_a : \R^m \to \R$ is a $1 \times m$ matrix whose $i$th entry is $\partial_i f(a)$. 
	\[ [df_a] = \begin{bmatrix} \partial_1 f(a) & \partial_2 f(a) & \dotsb & \partial_m f(a) \end{bmatrix} \]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
	Let $r$ be the ``error in approximation'' function
	\[ r(v) = f(a+v) - f(a) - df_a(v), \]
	so that $|r(v)| = o(|v|)$ as $h \to 0$. Then 
	\[ \begin{aligned} 0 &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|r(he_i)|}{|he_i|} \\
	&= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|f(a+he_i)-f(a)-df_a(he_i)|}{|h|} \\
	&= \lim_{h \to 0} \left| \frac{f(a+he_i)-f(a)}{h} - df_a(e_i) \right| \end{aligned} \]
	which means that $d f_a(e_i) = \partial f_a(e_i)$. 
\end{proof}

\begin{remark} \label{gradient}
	If $f : S \to \R$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$, then the vector \[ [df_a]^T = \begin{bmatrix} \partial_1 f(a) \\ \partial_2 f(a) \\ \vdots \\ \partial_m f(a) \end{bmatrix} \in \R^m \]
	is often called the \emph{gradient} of $f$ at $a$, and is denoted $\nabla f(a)$. 
\end{remark}

Now for the important caveat: unfortunately, the converse to \cref{differentiability-implies-partials} is \emph{not} true. 

\begin{exercise} \label{partials-dont-imply-differentiable}
	Consider the function $f : \R^2 \to \R$ defined as follows.
	\[ f(x_1, x_2) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{x_1 x_2}{x_1^2 + x_2^2} &  \text{if } (x_1, x_2) \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } (x_1, x_2) = 0 \end{cases} \]
	Show that $\partial_1 f(0)$ and $\partial_2 f(0)$ both exist, but that $f$ is not even continuous at 0, so cannot be differentiable by \cref{differentiable-implies-continuous}. \textit{Hint.} Approach the origin along the line $x_1 = x_2$. 
\end{exercise}

This behavior is fairly pathological; \cref{continuous-partials} later on will provide a converse to \cref{differentiability-implies-partials} under a fairly mild additional hypothesis. 

\subsection{Jacobian matrix}

\begin{definition}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$. The \emph{Jacobian matrix} at $a$, denoted $J(a)$, is the $n \times m$ matrix whose $(j, i)$-entry is $\partial_i f_j(a)$. In other words,
	\[ J(a) = \begin{bmatrix} \partial_1 f_1(a) & \partial_2 f_1(a) & \dotsb & \partial_m f_1(a) \\ 
	\partial_1 f_2(a) & \partial_2 f_2(a) & \dotsb & \partial_m f_2(a) \\
	\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
	\partial_1 f_n(a) & \partial_2 f_n(a) & \dotsb & \partial_m f_n(a) \end{bmatrix} \]
	Note that \cref{differentiable-by-components,differentiability-implies-partials} tell us that all of these partial derivatives exist. 
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem} \label{jacobian-matrix}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R^m$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$. Then \[ [df_a] = J(a). \]
\end{theorem}

\begin{exercise}
	Prove \cref{jacobian-matrix}. \textit{Hint.} Use \cref{differentiable-by-components,differentiability-implies-partials}. 
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Rank of a differentiable map}

\begin{definition}
	Suppose $f : S \to \R^n$ is differentiable at an interior point $a \in S$. The rank of the linear map $df_a : \R^m \to \R^n$ is also called the \emph{rank of $f$ at $a$}, denoted $\rank_a(f)$. Note that $\rank_a(f) \leq \min\{m,n\}$.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item If $\rank_a(f) = n$, then we say that $f$ is a \emph{submersion at $a$}. 
		\item If $\rank_a(f) = \min\{m,n\}$, then we say that $a$ is a \emph{regular point} of $f$. 
		\item If $\rank_a(f) < \min\{m,n\}$, then we say that $a$ is a \emph{critical point} of $f$.
	\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Note that, if $m \geq n$, then $f$ is a submersion at $a$ if and only if $a$ is a regular point of $f$. If $m < n$, then $f$ cannot be a submersion at any point. 

\begin{caution}
	A map $f$ being a submersion at an interior point $a \in S$ is always defined as it is above, and critical points are always defined to be the opposite of regular points. But some mathematicians use a different definition of regularity: they define a point to be regular if $\rank_a(f) = n$ (in which case, its always true that $f$ is a submersion at $a$ if and only if $a$ is a regular point of $f$). If $m \geq n$, there's no difference between these alternative definitions and our definitions. But, if $m < n$, there is a difference: these other mathematicians would say that all points are critical in this case, but that isn't true for us. So, if you see the words ``regular point'' and ``critical point'' in another text and it's possible that $m < n$, it's worth looking back to make sure you know what the author means!
\end{caution}

\section{Levels of differentiability}

Throughout, $U$ denotes an open subset of $\R^m$. 

\begin{definition}
	If a function $f : U \to \R^n$ is differentiable at every $a \in U$, then $f$ is \emph{differentiable on $U$}. Then the \emph{derivative} of $f$, denoted $df$, is the function $U \to \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n)$ given by $a \mapsto df_a$. Given $i = 1, \dotsc, m$ and $j = 1, \dotsc n$, the \emph{$i$th partial derivative} $\partial_i f_j$ is the function $U \to \R$ defined by $a \mapsto \partial_i f_j(a)$. 
\end{definition}

\subsection{Continuous differentiability}

At this point, we will need to make use of the operator norm; see \cref{operator-norm} if you have not seen this before. Under this norm, the set $\mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n)$ of all linear maps $\R^m \to \R^n$ can be regarded a metric space (cf. \cref{operators-as-metric-space}). 

\begin{definition}
	If $f : U \to \R^n$ is a differentiable function and the derivative $df : U \to \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n)$ is continuous, then $f$ is said to be \emph{continuously differentiable}. 
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma} \label{continuously-differentiable-by-components}
	A function $f : U \to \R^n$ is continuously differentiable if and only if the component functions $f_j : U \to \R$ are all continuously differentiable for $j = 1, \dotsc, n$. 
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
	We will use \cref{differentiable-by-components}, which tells us that $f$ is differentiable if and only if $f_j$ is differentiable for all $j = 1, \dotsc, n$, and moreover that $df_{j,a} = \pi_j \circ df_a$ for all $a \in U$. In other words, the following diagram commutes. 
	\[ \begin{tikzcd} U \ar{r}{df} \ar[bend right]{dr}[swap]{df_j} & \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n) \ar{d}{\pi_j \circ -} \\ & \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R) \end{tikzcd} \]
	You will verify in \cref{continuously-differentiable-by-components-exercise}(a) below that the map ``postcompose with $\pi_j$'' map $\pi_j \circ - : \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n) \to \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R)$ is continuous. Thus, if $f$ is continuously differentiable, then $df_j$ is the composite of the two continuous maps $df$ and $\pi_j \circ -$, so it is itself continuous. 
	
	Conversely, suppose that $df_j$ is continuous for all $j$. Let us show that $df$ is continuous at every $a \in U$. Suppose $\epsilon > 0$. Since $df_j$ is continuous, there exists $\delta_j > 0$ such that \[ \|df_{j,a} - df_{j,b}\| < \frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{n}} \] for all $b \in U$ such that $|a - b| < \delta_j$. Let $\delta := \min\{\delta_1, \dotsc, \delta_n\}$. Then $\|df_a - df_b\| < \epsilon$ for all $b \in U$ such that $|a-b| < \delta$, as you will verify in \cref{continuously-differentiable-by-components-exercise}(b) below. 
\end{proof}

\begin{exercise} \label{continuously-differentiable-by-components-exercise}
	\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
		\item Check that the ``postcompose with $\pi_j$'' map $\mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R^n) \to \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R)$ (ie, the one  given by $\phi \mapsto \pi_j \circ \phi$) is continuous. Alternatively, prove the more general statement given in \cref{composition-continuous} and then derive this statement.   
		\item With notation as in the latter part of the proof above, verify that $\|df_a - df_b\| < \epsilon$. 
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

The proof of the following theorem is surprisingly challenging. You may need to read through it several times. 

\begin{theorem} \label{continuous-partials}
	A function $f : U \to \R^n$ is continuously differentiable if and only if the partial derivatives $\partial_i f_j : U \to \R$ exist and are continuous for all $i = 1, \dotsc, m$ and $j = 1, \dotsc, n$. 
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
	Thanks to \cref{continuously-differentiable-by-components}, we can assume without loss of generality that $n = 1$. If $f$ is differentiable, then \cref{differentiability-implies-partials} tells us that $\partial_i f = df(e_i)$. In other words, the following diagram commutes.
	\[ \begin{tikzcd} U \ar{r}{df} \ar[bend right]{dr}[swap]{\partial_i f} & \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R) \ar{d}{\text{``evaluate at $e_i$''}} \\ & \R \end{tikzcd} \]
	The ``evaluate at $e_i$'' map is continuous by \cref{evaluation-continuous}. If $f$ is continuously differentiable, then $df$ is continuous, so we conclude that the composite $\partial_i f$ is also continuous. 
	
	The converse is the hard part of this proof. Recall from \cref{partials-dont-imply-differentiable} above that $\partial_i f$ existing for all $i$ is \emph{not} sufficient to guarantee differentiability; we will need to use the fact that $\partial_i f$ exists \emph{and is continuous} to conclude differentiability. 
	
	Fix $a \in U$. Let $\ell$ be the linear map $\R^m \to \R$ such that \[ [\ell] = \begin{bmatrix}
	\partial_1f(a) & \dotsb & \partial_m f(a)
	\end{bmatrix}. \]
	In other words, $\ell$ is given by
	\[ \ell(h_1, \dotsc, h_m) = h_1 \partial_1 f(a) + \dotsb + h_m \partial_m f(a). \]
	We will show that 
	\[ |f(a+v) - f(a) - \ell(v)| = o(|v|) \text{ as } v \to 0, \]
	which implies that $f$ is differentiable at $a$ and that $df_a = \ell$. In other words, we will show that, for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that, for all $v \in \R^m$ with $|v| < \delta$, we have
	\begin{equation} \label{continuous-differentiability-desired} |f(a+v) - f(a) - \ell(v)| \leq |v|\epsilon. \end{equation}
	
	Let $\delta_0 > 0$ be small enough that the open ball $B(a, \delta_0)$ is contained in $U$. We will soon make it smaller to get inequality \eqref{continuous-differentiability-desired} to hold. 
	Suppose $v = (h_1, \dotsc, h_m) \in \R^m$ with $|v| < \delta_0$. Then consider the following vectors in $\R^m$. 
	\[ \begin{aligned} 
	v_0 &= 0 \\
	v_1 &= (h_1, 0, 0 \dotsc, 0, 0) \\
	v_2 &= (h_1, h_2, 0, \dotsc, 0, 0) \\
	&~\,\vdots \\
	v_{m-1} &= (h_1, h_2, h_3, \dotsc, h_{m-1}, 0) \\
	v_m &= (h_1, h_2, h_3, \dotsc, h_{m-1}, h_m) = v \end{aligned} \]
	See \cref{continuous-differentiability-picture}. 
	Clearly $|v_i| \leq |v|$ for all $i$.

	\[ f(a+v) - f(v) = \sum_{i = 1}^m \left( f(a+v_i) - f(a+v_{i-1}) \right). \]
	Let us fix $i$ temporarily. Notice that $v_i = v_{i-1} + h_i e_i$, so
	\[ f(a+v_i) - f(a+v_{i-1}) = f(a+v_{i-1}+h_ie_i) - f(a+v_{i-1}). \]
	We now apply the mean value theorem \ref{mean-value-theorem} to the single variable function \[ h \mapsto f(a + v_{i-1} + he_i) \] on the closed interval between $0$ and $h_i$. For any such $h$, we have $|v_{i-1}+he_i| < \delta$, so the points $a+v_{i-1}+he_i$ are all contained inside the ball $B(a, \delta_0) \subseteq U$. Thus this function is well-defined and continuous. It is also differentiable on the interior of this interval: its derivative at a value $h$ is $\partial_i f(a+v_{i-1} + he_i)$, by definition of partial derivatives. The mean value theorem says that there exists $s_i$ between 0 and $h_i$ such that
	\[ \partial_i f(a+v_{i-1} + se_i) = \frac{f(a+v_{i-1} + h_ie_i) - f(a+v_{i-1})}{h_i} = \frac{f(a+v_{i}) - f(a + v_{i-1})}{h_i}, \]
	or, in other words,
	\[ f(a + v_i) - f(a+v_{i-1}) = h_i \partial_i f(a+v_{i-1}se_i). \]
	See \cref{continuous-differentiability-picture} again. Unfixing $i$, we find that
	\[ f(a+v) - f(a) = \sum_{i = 1}^m (f(a+v_i) - f(a+v_{i-1})) = \sum_{i = 1}^m h_i \partial_i f(a+v_{i-1} + s_i e_i). \]
	Thus
	\[ |f(a+v) - f(a)-\ell(v)| \leq \sum_{i = 1}^m |h_i| \cdot |\partial_i(a+v_{i-1}+s_ie_i) - \partial_if(a)|. \]
	Since $\partial_if$ is continuous, there exists $\delta_i > 0$ such that $|\partial_i(a+w) - \partial_i(a)| < \epsilon/m$ for all $|w| < \delta_i$. Let $\delta = \min\{\delta_0, \delta_1, \dotsc, \delta_m\}$. If $|v| < \delta$, then $|v_{i-1} + s_ie_i| < |v| < \delta$ for all $i$, so
	\[ |\partial_i(a+v_{i-1} + s_ie_i) - \partial_i f(a)| < \epsilon/m, \]
	which means that
	\[ |f(a+v) - f(a)-\ell(v)| \leq \sum_{i = 1}^m \frac{|h_i| \epsilon}{m} \leq |v|\epsilon. \]
	This proves that $f$ is differentiable at any point $a \in U$, which completes the hard part of this proof. The final step is to show that $df : U \to \mathscr{L}(\R^m, \R)$ is continuous. This is left for you to check; see \cref{continuous-partials-details} below. 
\end{proof}

\subsection{$C^k$ functions}

\begin{definition} \label{cn-multivar}
	We say that a function $f : U \to \R^n$ is $C^0$ if it is continuous. Then, inductively, for any positive integer $k$, we say that a function $f : U \to \R^n$ is $C^k$ if the partial derivatives $\partial_i f_j$ all exist and are $C^{k-1}$ functions $U \to \R$. 
\end{definition}

\Cref{continuous-partials} tells us that $f : U \to \R^n$ is $C^1$ if and only if it is continuously differentiable. It is clear from the definitions that a function $f : U \to \R^n$ is $C^k$ if and only if its component functions $f_j : U \to \R$ are $C^k$. 

\begin{exercise}
	Show that the set of all $C^k$ functions $U \to \R^n$ is a vector space. 
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
	Suppose $U$ is an open subset $\R^m$, that $V$ is an open subset of $\R^n$, and that $f : U \to V$ and $g : V \to \R^p$ are both $C^k$. Show that $g \circ f$ is also $C^k$. 
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
	Suppose $f, g : U \to \R$ are both $C^k$. Show that $fg$ is also $C^k$. 
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Equality of mixed partials}

\begin{definition}
	Suppose $f : U \to \R$ is $C^2$. The partial derivative $\partial_i f : U \to \R$ for any $i = 1, \dotsc, m$ is a continuously differentiable function, so its $j$th partial deriative $\partial_j( \partial_i f)$ is a continuous function $U \to \R$ for any $j = 1, \dotsc m$. We denote the function $\partial_j(\partial_i f)$ more succinctly as $\partial_{j,i} f$. 
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Equality of mixed partials] \label{mixed-partials}
	Suppose $f : U \to \R$ is $C^2$. Then, for all $i, j = 1, \dotsc, m$, we have \[ \partial_{j,i} f = \partial_{i,j} f. \] 
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Smooth functions}

\begin{definition} \label{smooth-multivar}
	We say that a function $f : U \to \R^n$ is $C^\infty$, or \emph{infinitely differentiable}, or \emph{smooth}, if it is $C^k$ for all $k$.
