Here's an observation about the Hindi-Urdu passive. 

# Background

Let's start off with some background information. The subject of such a Hindi-Urdu transitive verb (in the present progressive) occurs in the direct case, which will be abbreviated DIR in glosses below. The verb inflects to match the person, number, and gender of the subject. There are two options for the direct object. It can occur either in the direct case, or in the oblique followed by the postposition *ko*. I'll abbreviate the latter situation DAT in glosses below (since this construction otherwise corresponds most closely to dative cases in other languages). 

The two options for the direct object  are not entirely synonymous or interchangeable. For example, consider the following. Note that the word *naukrānī* is a feminine common noun referring to a female servant. 

<table width="100%">
<tr>
<td rowspan="3" width="15%">(A1)</td>
<td>akbar naukrānī bulā rahā hai</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar-DIR servant-DIR call-MASC-SG PROG-MASC-SG PRES-3rd-SG</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar is calling for a servant.</td></tr>

<tr>
<td rowspan="3">(A2)</td>
<td>akbar naukrānī-ko bulā rahā hai</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar-DIR servant-DAT call-MASC-SG PROG-MASC-SG PRES-3rd-SG</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar is calling for the servant.</td></tr>
</table>

The DAT marking on direct objects roughly indicates specificity; in other words, *naukrānī-ko* used as a direct object means something like "the servant" or "a specific servant." The details of the semantics are not important for us, but what is important is that proper nouns are certainly "specific" and therefore are obligatorily marked DAT when used as direct objects. 

<table width="100%">
<tr>
<td rowspan="2"  width="15%">(A3)</td>
<td>*akbar-ne jodhā bulāī</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar-ERG Jodha call-PAST-SG-FEM</td></tr>

<tr>
<td rowspan="3">(A4)</td>
<td>akbar-ne jodhā-ko bulāyā</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar-ERG Jodha-DAT call-PAST-SG-MASC</td></tr>
<tr><td>Akbar called Jodha.</td></tr>
</table>

# Passives: Data

Passives in Hindi-Urdu are formed by compounding a perfective participle with an inflected form of an auxiliary verb. The following four are all acceptable passive constructions. The interesting thing to note is that (P3) is acceptable, despite the fact that (A3) is not. 

<table width="100%">
<tr>
<td rowspan="3" width="15%">(P1)</td>
<td>naukrānī bulāī gaī</td></tr>
<tr><td>servant (be-called)-PAST-SG-FEM</td></tr>
<tr><td>A/The servant was called.</td>
<tr>

<tr>
<td rowspan="3">(P2)</td>
<td>naukrānī-ko bulāyā gayā</td></tr>
<tr><td>servant-DAT (be-called)-PAST-SG-MASC</td></tr>
<tr><td>The servant was called.</td>
<tr>

<tr>
<td rowspan="3">(P3)</td>
<td>jodhā bulāī gaī</td></tr>
<tr><td>Jodha (be-called)-PAST-SG-FEM</td></tr>
<tr><td>Jodha was called.</td>
<tr>

<tr>
<td rowspan="3">(P4)</td>
<td>jodhā-ko bulāyā gayā</td></tr>
<tr><td>Jodha-DAT (be-called)-PAST-SG-MASC</td></tr>
<tr><td>Jodha was called.</td>
<tr>
</table>
