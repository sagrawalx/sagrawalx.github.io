# Introduction

## Theoretical Framework

King begins with an overview of a theoretical framework that attempts to explain the rise of nationalism. The framework he adopts is Paul Brass's as presented in *Language, Religion, and Politics in North India* (1974), which in turn adapts Karl Deutsch's theory as presented in *Nationalism and Social Communication* (1951). In this framework, groups of people are of one of the following types: 

1. An *ethnic group* is a group of people who share some objective characteristic (such as language, or religion), and who do not give any importance to the characteristic that sets them apart as an ethnic group. 
2. An ethnic group becomes a *community* when it develops an awareness of its common identity. 
3. A community becomes a *nationality* when it acquires some level of political power. 

Various processes are at work when groups transition from one stage to another. 

* The process *social mobilization* roughly encompasses various processes of modernization (urbanization, rise in literacy, expansion of news media, etc). The socially mobilized portion of the group is brought into a network of communication with others in the group. 
* *Assimilation* is the tendency of the network of communication to be interconnected, and *differentiation* is the tendency for the network to bifurcate into separate subnetworks. These tendencies compete and determine whether ultimately one or many nationalities will arise. 
* *Multi-symbol congruence* refers to the process by which political elites choose one characteristic (or "symbol") as primary, and then strive to bring other characteristics into line with the primary one.

## North Indian Situation

In North India, Brass argues that political elites chose religion to be the primary characteristic along which to draw fault lines, and then strove to make language "congruent" with religion. One piece of this story is the Hindi movement of the late 1800s, and it is this piece on which King's book focuses. 

No Hindi movement existed until 1860. By 1900, however, the Hindi community had won at least one major political victory.
