*Reflections on my relationship with mathematics*

<hr/>
**About.** Inspired by my readings and conversations[^attributions] about education during the summer of 2018, I decided to assign a writing assignment for my [Fall 2018, Block 2 Linear Algebra class](/teaching/fa18-b2_ma220/) at Colorado College asking my students to reflect on their relationship with mathematics. To reciprocate their personal stories about mathematics, I decided to write my own mathematical autobiography to share with them.
<hr/>

[^attributions]: Some specific things that inspired this writing assignment were reading *Whistling Vivaldi* by Claude Steele, talking to Beth Malmskog over coffee, having discussions with several new faculty in my cohort during the New Faculty Orientation at Colorado College, [Steven Strogatz's tweet](https://twitter.com/stevenstrogatz/status/772859565725589504?lang=en) about this kind of a writing assignment, and the [post on Art of Mathematics](https://www.artofmathematics.org/blogs/cvonrenesse/mathematical-autobiographies) linked to in the comments on that tweet.

My family immigrated to the United States when I was a child, just old enough to remember the experience. I remember landing at SFO, leaving the airport in a car, and being absolutely awestruck by the highway overpasses crisscrossing over one another in every direction. Over the course of the next few months, I remember being just thrilled that I could take hot showers at any hour of the day. And I remember my father feeling like the math classes at me and my younger sister's new American elementary school weren't teaching us enough.

Concerned about our education, he started ordering us math textbooks from India and assigning us problems from these textbooks. Whenever we had a few idle minutes, he would start quizzing us with problems from these books. My sister and I weren't very happy about all of this; after all, our friends at school didn't have *extra* homework!

At some point, these Indian textbooks started introducing me to a bit of basic algebra. My younger sister, meanwhile, was learning about the basic arithmetic operations. On a family roadtrip around this time, my father began quizzing me with some algebra questions. I got a couple of them right, and my sister didn't like how well I seemed to be doing on this quiz. She broke in with a question of her own: "Oh yeah?! Well, what's two algebra four?" I didn't know the answer.

Thanks to my father's assignments, I was always well prepared for my math classes in grade school. I had some pretty good math teachers along the way too: people who could explain the *why* to me in ways that my textbooks from India didn't. I remember liking my math classes. When it came time to start doing my homework in the evenings, it was always my math homework that I'd do first.

But it wasn't something I really felt passionate about. 

I still don't know what "two algebra four" is, and my sister still refuses to tell me.
