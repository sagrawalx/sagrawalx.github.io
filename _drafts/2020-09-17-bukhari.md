Historians of Urdu literature begin their story with Amir Khusrau of Delhi, and after mentioning a few puzzles and riddles, they attribute a particular ghazal to him which begins, "*zi ḥāl-i-miskīn makun taġāful, durā'e nainā~ banā'e batiyā~*." Immediately after this, they arrive in the Deccan, hundreds of miles away from Delhi, and start to recite for us the writings of Bijapuri poets. But this story engenders an conundrum for literary history which is not easy to ignore. 

Amir Khusrau's Persian writings make evident that he did test his mettle by writing in "Hindi," but we find no internal or external evidence that the language which he called "Hindi" is the same language which was later named Urdu. Neither do we have any printed or handwritten book of his Hindi writings which we might use to make definitive decisions about his language. The aforementioned Hindi writing customarily continues to be attributed to him in spite of the fact that, so far, it is mere conjecture. Since Mahmud Sherani's research establishing that *Khāliq Bārī* was written not by Amir Khusrau but by a different Khusrau who lived during Alamgir's reign, Amir Khusrau's above-mentioned Hindi writing has also become dubious. 

This conjectural writing is also of a type that lends credence to doubts. For example, if his famous ghazal "*zi ḥāl miskīn*" is subjected to linguistic analysis, it emerges that it is not Urdu but Braj Bhasha. It is true that Mohammad Hussain Azad said that Urdu derives from Braj, but that happened before Mahmud Sherani wrote *Panjāb Me~ Urdū*. In this way, this ghazal has no rightful place in Urdu literary history. The puzzles and riddles need further research to determine if these, too, were the writings of the same Khusrau who wrote *Khāliq Bārī*. But perhaps they can be accomodated in literary history until they are established to be the works of someone else. 

Those historians of Urdu who follow up the story of Amir Khusrau with the writings of Bijapuri poets are entirely unable to explain the following: if Urdu was the ancient language of Delhi and a poet like Amir Khusrau was born into that linguistic milieu, then why did the tradition Urdu poetry suddenly die off in Delhi? According to  traditional history books, regular poetry in North India begins with the Delhi School, whose representatives are Arzu, Abru, Mazhar Jan-e-Janan, and so forth, but all of them were born 300 years after Amir Khusrau's death. The historians of Urdu who try to fill this historical void with the writing of Bijapuri poets don't tell us why Urdu poetry fell asleep after Amir Khusrau in the very city that is said to be Urdu's birthplace, and why it then suddenly awoke again after 300 years. 

Another aspect of this issue that is noteworthy is that the writings of Bijapuri poets that literary historians include in the history of Urdu are in not in Urdu but in the Bijapuri language. Certainly this will weigh heavy on certain dispositions, and they will refuse to accept it out of obstinacy or some other reasons, but this is in fact the truth. In the 18th issue of *Urdūnāmah* Karachi, I have conducted a detailed linguistic analysis to reveal the numerous differences between Urdu and Bijapuri. I have further suggested that the substance with which we have tried to fill the gulf of time in an attempt to set up a historical chronology is so weak that one cannot even stand atop it, leave alone walk on it. The long idleness of Delhi poets cannot be explained with the writings of Dakkani poets. 

At this juncture, the misunderstanding that is expressed is that the Bijapuri language is the ancient form of the same language whose modern form is our language. In other words, the relationship between our language and Bijapuri is that between modern and ancient. But perhaps those who say that Bijapuri is ancient are unaware of the fact that the language in which the ancient Bijapuri poets and writers expressed their thoughts 400 years ago is still spoken in Bijapur. People there still sing folk songs in this language and make us of it in their homes and markets. If this language deserves to be called "ancient," it also deserves to be called "modern." On the other hand, our language (Urdu) is ancient just as it is modern. In fact, we can find traces of this language not just 400 years ago, but even 600 years ago, as we will become apparent from the following discussion. It is strange that people say, on the one hand, that Amir Khusrau's puzzles and riddles (which are older than Dakkani poetry) are the first examples of Urdu, and on the other hand, that Dakkani is an ancient form of Urdu -- despite the fact that our language is much closer to Amir Khusrau's aforementioned writings than Dakkani! In light of this, it seems more appropriate to say that Amir Khusrau's language, rather than Dakkani, is ancient Urdu. 

Some Dakkani poets and writers have also expressed their belief that Dakkani and Urdu are two separate languages. For instance, in his book *Man Darpan* (written 1206 CE) which collects detailed information about the miracles of the Noble Prophet, Mohammad Baqir Agah of Delhi expresses this thought as follows: 

> *Agar bhāke me~ urdū ke mai~ kahtā*
> *Ko'ī us ko yahā~ ke logo~ se nah chahtā*
> 
> If I spoke in the language of Urdu
> No one here would like it

On page 29 of the January 1964 issue of the monthly *Qaumī Zabān* Karachi, this is explained as follows: 

> Āgāh ne use āsān dakhinī zabān me~ likhā hai, jise wuh urdū nahī~ kahte. is se sābit hotā hai kih un ke xayāl me~ dakhinī wa urdū do judā-judā zabāne~ hai~. 
> 
> Agah has written it in easy Dakkani, which he does not call Urdu. This proves that he believed Dakkani and Urdu to be two separate languages. 

The truth of the matter is that we have begun to maintain a peculiar attitude about Urdu literature: we only accept as "Urdu" that which was written in the Persian script, and the Persian script came into India alongside Muslims. For this reason, we do not include as a part of our literature the literature of the same language which had accumulated in Devanagari before the arrival of Muslims. On the other hand, there are older and more abundant examples of Bijapuri literature in the Persian script than of Urdu literature. This is because it took hundreds of years after the arrival of Muslims in India for Urdu to be written in the Persian script, whereas the local vernacular in Bijapur was written in the Persian script right away. In Northern India, Persian remained the sole  language of governmental and official functions until 1832 CE. Urdu was given much less attention in the beginning than Persian, since the latter had governmental support. The little Urdu that was collected was the result of individuals working alone. In contrast, the Bijapuri language received governmental sanction in the Deccan from the very  beginning, so it was patronized by both the government and by the people. In short, the shared script engendered a misunderstanding in our literature and we began to call Bijapuri literature ancient Urdu. 

This orthography-based view of Urdu literature is entirely mistaken from a linguistic perspective, because language is a soul whose body is orthography. Just as the essense of the soul is unaffected by changes in the body, so too is the reality of a language unaffected by changes in orthography. The orthography-based view presents to us a contradiction in our beliefs: we regard as Persian not just the literature written in the Arabic script, but also that which had accumulated thousands of years before the arrival of Muslims. But, when it comes to Urdu, we change our position and declare all of the literature in Urdu written in Devanagari to be Sanskrit or Hindi or something else and remove it from our histories. We do not think that we can find fragments of pure Urdu before the arrival of Muslims in India in the same way that we can find samples of pure Persian in Ancient Persia before the arrival of Muslims, and that these fragments form as important a component of Urdu literary history as ancient Persian samples form of Persian literary history. 

This deficiency in literary history has caused many misunderstandings about the beginnings of Urdu. Critics and historians established the identity and history of the language on the basis only of literature in the Persian script. Thus, Urdu is commonly thought to begin during the reign of Shah Jahan, when the language was named *Zabān-e-Urdū* ("the Language of the Camp") in reference to the *Urdū-e-Mu'allā* ("The Exalted Camp"). Sample documents start appearing even later than that. Historians do regard Amir Khusrau as the first Urdu poet, but they fail to provide any examples of North Indian Urdu poetry between Amir Khusrau and the Delhi Ancients[^ancients]. The reason for this is that the documents from this intermediate period are found in the Devanagari script, and these historians either did not see these documents or ignored them because they only accept documents written in the Persian script to be Urdu. In such a situation, it is boundlessly important to search for Devanagari manuscripts and printed books, because this is that it will be possible to set up a correct literary history. 

[^ancients]: The "Delhi Ancients" (*qudamā-e-dihlī*) are the early poets of the Delhi School. 

In fact, Urdu and Hindi are two forms of the same language, which lingui
sts have named "Khari Boli." There are two differences in their present customs. The first is orthography, and the second is vocabulary. Hindi is written in Devanagari and consequently has been flooded with Sanskrit loans. Urdu, being written in the Persian script, has loaned countless Perso-Arabic words. But these differences are negligible  from the perspective of linguistics, because they do not affect the fundamental identity of a language. From this point of view, Hindi and Urdu have the same history. The history of Khari Boli is an important part of Urdu, just as it is of Hindi, and it must be regarded as ancient Urdu despite the presence of loanwords from Sanskrit and other contemporaneous languages. 

In the following, I present some ancient samples of this Khari Boli which are inseparable parts of our literature. I have chosen just those bits which have few Sanskrit loans and many Perso-Arabic loans. If Sanskrit loans appear abundant in some places in spite of this, this should be deemed a deficiency of my search rather than of the samples themselves. I did not mention some poets, despite the fact that they were Khari Boli poets, simply because I did not have access to their writing. Besides them, I'm certain I have excluded many poets whose work requires further research. In other words, until all of those poets' works are before us, we cannot place samples in their historical context and we cannot form correct judgments about this literature. 

In the preamble to *Bāg-o-Bahār*, Mir Amman begins the story of Urdu in Akbar's reign, but I begin my story in Amir Khusrau's era who our historians deem to be Urdu's first poet. In Amir Khusrau's era, a dervish named Gyaneshvar lived in Marhatta. He was born 4 *kosas* from present-day Paithan, in a village on the north bank of the Godawari River, in 1275 CE. Four books of his writings are found today, out of which his commentary on the Bhagavat Gita, called the *Gyaneshvarī*, is the most famous. Here we record a sample of his Urdu writing. 

> सोई कच्चा वे कच्चा, वे नहीं गुरु का बच्चा।
> दुनिया तजकर खाक लगाई, जाकर बैठा बन माें।
> खेचरि मुद्रा बज्रासन मों, ध्यान धरत है मन माें॥१॥
> तीरथ करके ऊमर खोई, जोग जगत में सारी।
> धन, कामिनि औ' कंजर त्यागे, जोग कमाया भारी॥२॥
> गुप्त होयकर परगट होवे, गोकुल, मथुरा, कासी।
> सिद्ध हुए जी प्राण जुु निकले, सत्य लोक के बासी॥३॥
> शास्त्रों में तो नहीं रहा कुछ, पुरान गायन माया।
> भेद विधि का मारग चलता, तनका लकड़ा काया॥४॥
> कुण्डलिनी कूं खूब चढ़ावे, ब्रह्मरंध्र को जावे।
> चलता है पानी के ऊपर, बोलत सोई होवे॥५॥
> हुकुम निवृत्ति का ज्ञानेश्वर कूं, तिनको ऊपर जाना।
> सद्गुरु की जहँ कृपा भई, तहँ आपहि आप पिछाना॥६॥

Some words in this sample are Sanskrit. It is natural to find Sanskrit words in Indian languages due to the shared script of Devanagari. Besides this, a few words of Braj Bhasha are also found, and this is due to the fact that Braj and Urdu were both connected to Maharashtrians and so the two languages enjoyed both regional and temporal proximity. Another reason is that Gyaneshwar was a Bhakti poet, and the language of Bhakti poets is often variegated[^variegated]. In any case, ignoring these few words, the entire poem is built around the framework of Urdu, and it even includes a few Perso-Arabic words which prove the influence of Muslims on Indian languages in this early era. Compare this poem with the writings of Bijapuri poets who lived after Gyaneshvar and decide whose language deserves to be called "Urdu." 

[^variegated]: This mixed language is called [*sadhukkaṛī*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadhukkadi) or [*sant bhāṣā*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sant_Bhasha). 

Gyaneshvar had a sister, Mukta Bai, who was four years younger than him and who was also a Marathi language poet. Consider the influence of Urdu on her language as well. Here is the sample. 

> वाह वाह साहबजी सद्गुरु लाल गुसाईंजी,
> लाल बीच में उदला काला ओंठ पीठ सों काला।
> पीत उन्मनी भ्रमर गुंफा रस झूलनेवाला,
> सहस्रदल मों उलखली खाय आज लों परमाना।
> जहाँ-तहाँ साधु दसवा आपहि आप ठिकाना।
> सद्गुरु चेले दोनों बराबर एक दस्त मों भाई,
> एक से ऐसे दरसन पाए महाराज मुक्ताबाई।

From here begins a long chain of Bhakti poets. The first of these is Namdev, who was a contemporary of Gyaneshvar and was born into a family of tailors. Consider his Urdu writing: 

> मैं अंधुले की टेक तेरा नाम खुंदकारा।
> मैं गरीब मैं मिसकीन तेरा नाम है अधारा॥

> आजु नामे बीठल देखा, मूरख को समझाऊँ रे॥
> पांडे तुमरी गायत्री लोधे का खेतु खाती थी।
> लैकरि ठेगा टाँगरी तोरी, लांगत लांगत जाती थी॥
> पांडे तुमरा महादेव धौले बलद, चढ़ाया आवत देखा था।
> मोदी के घर खांडा पाका, वाका लड़का मारा था॥
> पांडे तुमरा रामचंद, सोभी आवत देखा था।
> रावन सेती सरबर होई, घर की जोई गँवाई थी॥ (?????)

After Namdev, Kabir was the next famous name in Bhakti literature. He was born into a family of weavers in Banaras, and died in Maghar. There is intense debate among historians about the years of his life, but we can place him between 1399 and 1518 CE as he was a contemporary of Sikandar Lodhi. Here are some samples of his writing: 

> सब्द[^sabd] की चोट लगी मेरे मन में, बेध गया तन सारा।
> औषध-मूल कुछ नहीं लागै, का करै बैद बेचारा॥
> 
> The word has injured my heart and pierced my body. 
> No medicine has any effect. What can the poor doctor do?


[^sabd]: Translator's note: Bukhari's text says برہ here, but I have been unable to find a Devanagari text which says anything like this. 

> हाड़ जले जैसे लकड़ी का तूला, केस जले जैसे घास का पूला।
> 
> Bones burn like sticks of wood, hair burns like bundles of straw

> जाति न पूछो साधु की, पूछि लीजिए ज्ञान।
> मोल करो तरवार का, पड़ा रहन दो म्यान॥
> 
> Don't ask about a sadhu's caste, ask instead about his knowledge
> Weigh the sword itself, leave aside it's sheath

> बेद-कतेब इफतरा भाई दिल का फिकर न जाई।
> टुक दम करारी जो करहु हाजिर हजूर खुदाई॥
> 
> The Holy Books are nonsense, brother, they don't ease the heart's worry
> He who calms himself for just a few moments finds all of creation before him
