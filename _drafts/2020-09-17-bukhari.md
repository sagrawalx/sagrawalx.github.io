Historians of Urdu literature begin their story with Amir Khusrau of Delhi, and after mentioning a few puzzles and riddles[^1], they attribute a particular ghazal to him which begins, "*zi ḥāl-i-miskīn makun taġāful, durā'e nainā~ banā'e batiyā~*." Immediately after this, they arrive in the Deccan, hundreds of miles away from Delhi, and start to recite for us the writings of Bijapuri poets. But this story engenders an conundrum for literary history which is not easy to ignore. 

[^1]: What is du-suxan?

Amir Khusrau's Persian writings make evident that he did test his mettle by writing in "Hindi," but we find no internal or external evidence that the language which he called "Hindi" is the same language which was later named Urdu. Neither do we have any printed or handwritten book of his Hindi writings which we might use to make definitive decisions about his language. The aforementioned Hindi writing customarily continues to be attributed to him in spite of the fact that, so far, it is mere conjecture. Since Mahmud Sherani's research establishing that *Khāliq Bārī* was written not by Amir Khusrau but by a different Khusrau who lived during Alamgir's reign, Amir Khusrau's above-mentioned Hindi writing has also become dubious. 

This conjectural writing is also of a type that lends credence to doubts. For example, if his famous ghazal "*zi ḥāl miskīn*" is subjected to linguistic analysis, it emerges that it is not Urdu but Braj Bhasha. It is true that Mohammad Hussain Azad said that Urdu derives from Braj, but that happened before Mahmud Sherani wrote *Panjāb Me~ Urdū*. In this way, this ghazal has no rightful place in Urdu literary history. The puzzles and riddles need further research to determine if these, too, were the writings of the same Khusrau who wrote *Khāliq Bārī*. But perhaps they can be accomodated in literary history until they are established to be the works of someone else. 

Those historians of Urdu who follow up the story of Amir Khusrau with the writings of Bijapuri poets are entirely unable to explain the following: if Urdu was the ancient language of Delhi and a poet like Amir Khusrau was born into that linguistic milieu, then why did the tradition Urdu poetry suddenly die off in Delhi? According to  traditional history books, regular poetry in North India begins with the Delhi School, whose representatives are Arzu, Abru, Mazhar Jan-e-Janan, and so forth, but all of them were born 300 years after Amir Khusrau's death. The historians of Urdu who try to fill this historical void with the writing of Bijapuri poets don't tell us why Urdu poetry fell asleep after Amir Khusrau in the very city that is said to be Urdu's birthplace, and why it then suddenly awoke again after 300 years.
