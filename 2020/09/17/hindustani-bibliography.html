Hindi-Urdu Annotated Bibliography (2020-09-17)

Here are a few references that deal with the history of Hindi-Urdu (also called Hindustani, Khari Boli, …). They are ordered alphabetically by the last name of the author. Included below each one are brief summaries and/or relevant excerpts. Please let me know if you find any broken links or other problems of any kind.

  • BANGHA, Imre. “Rekhta: Poetry in Mixed Language: The Emergence of Khari Boli Literature in North India.” In Before the Divide: Hindi and Urdu Literary Culture, edited by Francesca Orsini. New Delhi: Orient Blackswan, 2010.

    • This article points out several interesting aspects of the early history of Khari Boli. The main thrust is a discussion of manuscripts containing Khari Boli. While some texts are attested as examples of Khari Boli as early as the 1200s, there are no known manuscripts surviving from this era and the philological record suggests that later manuscripts may have altered the original texts. The earliest verifiable manuscripts containing Khari Boli go back perhaps to the late 1400s or early 1500s. These earliest specimens are written in the Perso-Arabic script. In the 1600s, a tradition developed of writing Khari Boli in Nagari (and related scripts such as Gurmukhi), especially among Nirgun sants. It goes without saying that Persian loans permeate these texts. Texts of this tradition were ignored by the Hindi Language Movement of the late 1800s and beyond (perhaps since their efforts were largely focused on purging Khari Boli of its Persian loans?). Bangha further points out that these texts are also ignored in some histories of Urdu, such as S. R. Faruqi’s “Early Urdu Literary Culture and History.”

      Another interesting aspect of this article is a classification of types of language mixing in early Khari Boli poetry. One type in this classification is poetry written with a Khari Boli grammatical base, with appropriate Persian words inserted into that base; this is recognizable as the same kind of language mixing that one sees in modern Hindustani. The author calls other types of language mixing “macaronic.” These other types of mixing involve entire phrases or lines of Persian (in other words, macaronic poetry overtly exhibits Persian syntax).

      A third interesting observation the article makes is that, in the early literature, Khari Boli was used to “invoke a domestic female tone as opposed to the male and the non-domestic female world of the ghazals.” I found this interesting because it contrasts with later male-chauvinist attitudes of linguistic chauvinists of the Hindi Language Movement (eg, Suryakant Tripathi “Nirala”), who felt that Hindi (ie, Sanskritized Khari Boli) was a much more “virile” language than other Indo-Aryan languages with strong literary traditions (eg, Bengali and Braj).

  • FARUQI, Shamsur Rahman. Early Urdu Literary Culture and History. New Delhi: Oxford University Press, 2001. Chapter 1. [Link]

    • The author spends some time discussing language name, citing a number of names used by natives (including Hindī and Rek͟htah) and a number of names coined by the British (including Hindustani). In the late 1700s, it appears that Hindī was more often used to refer to the language, with Urdū or Urdū-e mu’allā referring to a place (the city of Delhi, the Mughal court, etc); this situation persists perhaps as late as the mid 1800s, since Shakespear’s 1849 dictionary does not recognize Urdū as a language name. The more complete Zabān-e urdū-e mu’allā referred originally to Persian, but perhaps began referring to Hindustani under the reign of Shah ’Alam II in the late 1700s, since he had a particular predilection for the language. The word Urdū as a language name took a while to establish, and it took even longer for Hindī and Urdū to be used contrastively.

      There is a narrative that the Arabic and Persian elements of the language was an artefact of “Muslim invaders and conquerors.” The author objects strongly to this narrative, and cites many examples of its ubiquity in British writings. It appears for the first time in native writing in Mīr Amman’s Bāġ-o-bahār (1803-1804). This text was intended mostly for a European audience, and appears to have misled otherwise competent and well-intentioned linguists (such as Grierson). Rather, the truer narrative is that the language, together with all of its indigenized Arabic and Persian elements, arose out of interactions of diverse people from all walks of life (scholars, poets, elite, plebian, etc).

      The author points out finally that heavily Sanskritized Hindi was resisted as late as the mid 1800s by educated Hindu speakers, and that the Sanskritization was partly an effort of the British.

  • GRIERSON, George Abraham. Imperial Gazetteer of India, Volume 1, Chapter VII. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1909. [URL]

    • This chapter very broadly describes the state of Indian philology in the early 1900s. One observation the author makes is that language names (as distinct from dialect names) are a chiefly European construction. He classifies Hindustani as a dialect of Western Hindi (alongside the other dialects of Haryanvi, Braj, Kanauji, and Bundeli). Urdu and Hindi are called “forms” of Hindustani. The author points out that the importance of this dialect arises not from its population of native speakers (who are outnumbered by those of Bengali), but from its prominent role in the subcontinental marketplace. The author asserts that the “Persian element was first introduced in excess” by Hindu officials in the Mughal court. The author also attributes the origins of Urdu prose, and of the Sanskritized Hindi that ejects words of Perso-Arabic origins, to Fort William College.

  • LAHIRI, Madhumati (2015). “An Idiom for India.” Interventions. [DOI]

    • This article describes the evolution of the “Hindustani project,” starting from the early days of British presence in the subcontinent, through the present. According to the author, the project begins with linguist Scottish linguist John Gilchrist’s distrust for grandiloquent native teachers and their penchant for obscure loans from Sanskrit, Persian, and Arabic (the author notes the racist tendencies of Gilchrist, but does not attribute blame for the Hindi-Urdu fissure to the British). The next phase is dominated by the political aspirations of the Indian National Congress and the Progressive Writers’ Movement. This includes Nehru’s vision of Basic Hindustani, the PWM’s vision of “a common language (Hindustani) and a common script (Indi-Roman) for all of India,” and Premchand’s Hindustanī zubān. In the postcolonial period, the author argues that, despite official “erasure” of Hindustani on the political front, the Hindustani project survives largely through Bollywood cinema and its market aspirations. The author argues that the language of Bollywood is an “imaginary,” a language which does not exist anywhere but which allows access to the largest possible audience. Quotes by Khwaja Ahmad Abbas play a prominent role in this section of the paper.

  • RUSHDIE, Salman. ‘The Art of Fiction No. 186.’ Interview with Jack Livings. Paris Review, 2005. [URL]

    INTERVIEWER: Bombay has many languages. What is your mother tongue?

    RUSHDIE: Urdu. Urdu is literally my mother’s tongue. It’s my father’s tongue, too. But in northern India one also spoke Hindi. Actually, what we spoke was neither of them, or rather more like both. I mean, what people in northern India actually speak is not a real language. It’s a colloquial mixture of Hindi and Urdu called Hindustani. It isn’t written. It’s the language of Bollywood movies. And some mixture of Hindustani and English is what we spoke at home.
