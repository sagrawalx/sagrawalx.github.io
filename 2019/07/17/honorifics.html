Hindustani honorific verbs (2019-07-17)

One often sees discussions of honorific verbs in Japanese. They come in two varieties: the respectful honorifics (尊敬語), which exalt the subject of the verb, and the humble honorifics (謙譲語), which express humility of the subject. For example, the verb one would normally use to talk about eating is taberu (食べる). But if one were speaking about one’s boss eating, one might use the respectful variant meshiagaru (召し上がる). On the other hand, one might talk about oneself eating in the presence of one’s boss using the humble variant itadaku (いただく).

It occurs to me that this phenomenon of honorific verbs of both varieties exists to some extent in Hindustani (ie, Urdu-Hindi) as well, though these expressions are not very common in the language as I’ve been exposed to it colloquially. They sound very formal; some of them evoke images of royal courts of a bygone era. In any case, I felt like it might be useful for me at some point if I compiled a list of the honorific verbal expressions I’ve encountered. I’ll continue to update this list as I find more.

Latest update: 2021-01-27

Respectful Honorifics

Devanagari Urdu Gloss
पधारना پدھارنا to come, go; to sit
फ़रमाना فرمانا to say; to do
तशरीफ़ लाना تشریف لانا to come
तशरीफ़ ले जाना تشریف لے جانا to go
तशरीफ़ रखना تشریف رکھنا to sit
तशरीफ़ फ़रमाना تشریف فرمانا to come, go
नोश फ़रमाना نوش فرمانا to eat, drink
शौक़ फ़रमाना شوق فرمانا to help oneself, do as one pleases
क़दम-रंजा करना (फ़रमाना) قدم رنجہ کرنا (فرمانا) to come, go1
अता करना (फ़रमाना) عطا کرنا (فرمانا) to give, bestow

One key productive principle at work here is use of the verb farmānā. In isolation, it often roughly means “to say,” but it can replace the karnā of some compound verbs to form honorific expressions. Some compound verbs using karnā (such as atā karnā) are already honorific; in this case, the farmānā substitution feels like it makes the expression all the more respectful.

Humble Honorifics

Devanagari Urdu Gloss
नज़र करना نذر کرنا to give
नज़र-ए-ख़िदमत करना نذر خدمت کرنا to give
हाज़िर-ए-ख़िदमत होना حاضر خدمت ہونا to be present
रवाना-ए-ख़िदमत करना روانۂ خدمت کرنا to send
इरसाल-ए-ख़िदमत करना ارسال خدمت کرنا to send
पेश-ए-ख़िदमत करना پیش خدمت کرنا to present

A key productive principle at work here is an izāfat compound using khidmat (“service”). The result is expressions which carry the sense of “in your service.” In the case of nazar karnā, which is already humble, the use of this izāfat perhaps adds extra humility.

Another point to note is that humble expressions are often expressed passively (using honā instead of the karnā that is listed above), perhaps in order to avoid mentioning the self altogether.

  1. Qadam is a “step” and ranjā is “troubled.” The expression qadam-ranjā karnā thus means something like “to take the trouble to take a step.”↩︎
