My Name (2018-08-20)

Some information about my names

Here is lots of information about my names (last name, first name, and nickname). The inspiration for this was Kiran Kedlaya's page about his name — and, of course, an overwhelming desire to procrastinate.

Last Name

Orthography

I write my last name as follows.

  • Latin alphabet: Agrawal
  • Nagari: अग्रवाल
  • Urdu alphabet: اگ‬‬روال

The orthographic variants of this last name are quite numerous. I don't think there should be any variants in the Urdu alphabet (aside from those that add the optional diacritics), but in Nagari, one encounters अगरवाल, and in the Latin alphabet, one sees both Agarwal and Aggarwal.

In fact, all three of these Latin alphabet variants occur in my close family. My grandfather spells his last name Aggarwal. Originally, my father and his sisters did too, but my father and one of his sisters have first names whose Latin alphabet spellings have 5 letters. As Aggarwal has 8 letters, this brought the total number of letters in their names to 13. Apparently one of their schoolteachers felt that them having 13 letter names was extremely inauspicious and insisted on them changing the spellings of their last name. For whatever reason, they both changed it differently: my father chose Agrawal while my aunt chose Agarwal.

In Japanese, I like writing アグラワール.1

Etymology

The last name seems etymologically to be composed of two morphemes. The first derives from Sanskrit agra (अग्र), roughly meaning "foremost," and the second appears to be a truncation of the Hindustani वाला · والا, roughly meaning "one who is/does."

In fact, one still occasionally runs into variants of the last name that retain the the terminal /aː/, though it is a bit less common; also, some early texts write the plural with a terminal /eː/ (as in अगरवाले · اگروالے).

Pronunciation

The orthographic variations in both the Latin alphabet and in Nagari reflect more genuine variations in the Hindustani pronunciation as well: sometimes it is /əgəɾvaːl/, and sometimes it is /əgɾəvaːl/.2

The anglicization of my last name I hear most often is /'ægɹəwɑːl/.

Mythology

There appears to be some mythology regarding my surname. It's slightly odd mythology in that we seem to be able to pin down the concoction of these myths.

Bharatendu Harishchandra (भरतेंदु हरिश्चंद्र · بھرتیندو حرشچندر, 1850-1885) wrote a short essay in 1871 entitled अगरवालों की उत्पत्ति · اگروالوں کی اتپتی ("The Genesis of the Agrawals"). In it, he describes an elaborate genealogy3 for the Agrawals and says that his stories are "compiled from the hearsay of tradition and from ancient texts."4 My understanding is that there has been no textual corroboration of his stories. Perhaps he drew primarily from "the hearsay of tradition," but I'd wager that he just made the stories up himself.

"The Language of the Agrawals"

In my opinion, the most interesting aspect of Harishchandra's essay is the following bit, found towards the beginning in the introduction:

[अगरवालों की] बोली स्त्री और पुरुष सब की खड़ी बोली अर्थात् उर्दू है।
اگروالوں کی] بولی ستری اور پرش سب کی کھڑی بولی ارتھات اردو ہے]
The language of the all of the Agrawals, both the men and women, is Khari Boli, i.e., Urdu.

This shows that, even as late as the late 1800s, the "Hindi is for Hindus and Urdu is for Muslims" propaganda campaign5 had not taken root completely — not even in the mind of Harishchandra, a self-described "Hindu traditionalist."

First Name

Orthography

My first name is written as follows.

  • Latin alphabet: Shishir
  • Nagari: शिशिर
  • Urdu alphabet: ششر

There are no orthographic variants that I am aware of.

In Japanese, probably シシル is the only reasonable transcription.

Pronunciation

The usual Hindustani pronunciation is /ʃɪʃɪɾ/.

The sound /ɪɾ/ does not occur naturally in English, and the typical anglicization is /ʃɪ'ʃɪəɹ/, rhyming with "sheer." Having grown up mostly in the United States, I have grown rather used to the anglicized pronunciation and respond readily to it.

Etymology: Sanskrit Onwards

My name descends from Sanskrit word śiśira (शिशिर), which functions both as the root of both a masculine noun6 and an adjective. As a noun, Apte's dictionary [1] gives the following definitions.

  1. Dew, hoarfrost
  2. The cold season, comprising the two months of māgha and phālguna.
  3. Coldness, frigidity

The precise boundaries of the months of māgha (माघ) and phālguna (फाल्गुन)7 depend on the calendar being used (as there seem to be several slightly different versions of the Hindu calendar), but the two month period ranges roughly from mid-January to mid-March (which is invariably a period that includes my birthday).

The various meanings for śiśira as an adjective that Apte lists can be more or less summarized with the single English adjective "cold."

The word propagates into other languages from Sanskrit, both Indo-Aryan and not. Sometimes it is loaned as is (ie, as a tatsama word), and sometimes it has undergone various phonological changes (ie, a tadbhava form is used).

  • In Pali, the tadbhava form sisira सिसिर is used; note that, as in Sanskrit, final schwas are not deleted in Pali. Pali dictionaries mention all of the Sanskrit meanings except "dew, hoarfrost."
  • In Hindustani, both the tatsama शिशिर · ششر and the tadbhava सिसिर · سسر occur, with the former being much more common in modern times. The word usually refers to the winter, broadly interpreted (ie, not necessarily the specific two month period of the Hindu calendar mentioned above).8
  • Bengali inherits the tatsama word শিশির, and it usually refers to dew or frost.9
  • Here are a couple of loans into non-Indo-Aryan languages.
    • Telugu శిశిరము ("cold; dew; frost; winter"), from the Sanskrit.
    • Khmer សិសិរៈ ("winter"), from the Pali.

Etymology: Before Sanskrit

Tracing the etymology of words back further than Sanskrit is difficult, as this leads us into the prehistoric era, but there are reasonable hypotheses. Linguists attest a Proto-Indo-European (PIE) root *ḱel, which meant something like "cold; cool; warm" (in other words, just not "hot"...?).10 It is thought that the Sanskrit śiśira descends from this PIE root [4].

This proposal may seem slightly strange to the unacquainted, but there are some general phonological phenomena that account for this hypothesis.

  • The PIE phoneme *ḱ is hypothesized to have become ś (श) in Sanskrit, while it became /k/ in Latin. Some examples of this phenomenon include the following.

    • Compare Latin equus and aśvas (अश्वस्), both meaning "horse" and conjecturally deriving from a PIE *eḱwos.
    • Compare Latin centum and Sanskrit śatam (शतम्), both meaning "hundred" and conjecturally deriving from a PIE *ḱm̥tóm.

    This is a particular case of the palatovelar stops of PIE becoming assibilated in the eastern satem branch of Indo-European.11

  • PIE is hypothesized to have had two liquid phonemes *l and *r which have been quite faithfully preserved as /l/ and /r/ in most Indo-European languages. But there seems to have been substantial confusion regarding these phonemes in Indo-Iranian. For example, both phonemes merged to /r/ in Avestan, and there seems to have been dialectical variation between /r/ and /l/ in Indo-Aryan. It's possible that the two liquids had merged to /r/ in Indo-Iranian, before differentiation of Indo-Aryan and Iranian, and that the phoneme /l/ was reintroduced into Indo-Aryan as a result of contact with non-Indo-European inhabitants of the Subcontinent. 12

Assuming this is true, my first name may share etymological history with all of the following.

  • The reconstructed Proto-Indo-Iranian *ĉarHád ("autumn; year"), which is the source of the following.
    • The Sanskrit noun śarat (शरत्, meaning "autumn; year").
      • It is loaned tatsama as শরত in Bengali, usually meaning "autumn."
      • In Hindustani, the Sanskrit is loaned tatsama as both शरद · شرد and शरत · شرت.13 There is also the tadbhava सरद · سرد. All of these usually mean "autumn."
    • Persian سال ("year"), which in turn is the source of the Hindustani साल · سال ("year").
  • The Persian adjective سرد ("cold").
    • This in turn is the source of the Hindustani adjective सर्द · سر ("cold; indifferent") and the noun सर्दी · سردی ("coldness; winter; the common cold").
  • The Lithuanian adjective šáltas ("cold").14
  • The reconstructed Proto-Germanic noun *hehlǭ ("hoarfrost").15 This is the attested source of the following nouns, meaning "hoarfrost."
    • Old Norse héla, from which derives its Icelandic homograph.
    • Old English heolca.
  • The reconstructed Proto-Germanic adjective *hlēwaz ("lukewarm"). This is attested as the source of the following.
    • English lew ("sunny; warm; tepid"), and possibly also the first part of lukewarm.
    • German lau ("lukewarm").
    • Dutch lauw ("lukewarm; cold; indifferent; relaxing").
    • Old Norse hlær ("warm; mild").
  • The Latin verb caleo ("to be warm or hot") and its many other derivative forms (eg, the noun calor ("warmth")). These lead to numerous descendents in Romance languages and in English.
    • French chaleur ("heat"), chaud ("hot"), ...
    • Spanish calor ("heat"), caliente ("hot"), ...
    • English calorie, ...

The Latin caleo descendents are quite surprising; they seem to be essentially polar antonyms to the Sanskrit śiśira!

It's worth noting that there's a separate PIE root *gel ("cold"), which is the source of Latin gelu and the English word cold.16 It may also be worth noting that the "hoar" of "hoarfrost" is also of a different root: that "hoar" is the same "hoar" that is found in "hoary," and comes to us via Proto-Germanic *hairaz meaning "gray; old," which in turn is from a PIE *ḱēy(w)- or *ḱyē(w)- meaning "gray, dark."

Literary references

Here are some occurrences of my first name in literature, organized by language. First up, we have Classical Sanskrit.

  • The epic poem Buddhacarita (बुद्धचरित) , by the 1st century Sanskrit poet Aśvaghoṣa (अश्वघोष), describes the life of the Buddha. In it, immediately after the Buddha proclaims that he has escaped the cycle of reincarnation and will not be born again, there is a verse as follows (the English translation here is due to E. B. Cowell).17

    खात् प्रस्रुते चंद्रमरीचिशुभ्रे द्वे वारिधारे शिशिरोष्णवीर्ये।
    शरीरसंस्पर्शसुखान्तराय निपेततुर मूर्धनि तस्य सौम्ये॥

    khāt prasrute candramarīciśubhre dve vāridhāre śiśiroṣṇavīrye
    śarīrasaṃsparśasukhāntarāya1 nipetatur mūrdhani tasya saumye

    Two streams of water bursting from heaven, bright as the moon’s rays, having the power of heat and cold, fell down upon that peerless one’s benign head to give refreshment to his body.

  • The poem R̥tusaṁhāra (ऋतुसंहार, "The Medley of Seasons"), by 4th-5th century Sanskrit poet Kālidāsa (कालिदास), describes natural phenomena characteristic of each of the six classical Indian seasons, and the reaction of lovers to these natural phenomena. Chapter 5 is dedicated to the season śiśira, and here are some of the characteristics Kālidāsa describes.

    • The demoiselle crane18 is known to migrate over the Himalayas in order to overwinter in the Indian subcontinent. Kālidāsa says this season is made...

      क्वचित्स्थितक्रौञ्चनिनादराजितम्
      kvacitsthitakrauñcaninādarājitam
      resplendent by the resounding cry of demoiselle crane roosting nearby somewhere.19

    • It's of course a cold time of year, with long nights. The poet describes

      वायवः सान्द्रतुषारशीतला
      vāyavaḥ sāndratuṣāraśītalā
      breezes chilled by dense frost,

      nights

      तुषारसंघातनिपातशीतलाः शशाङ्कभाभिः शिशिरीकृतः पुनः
      tuṣārasaṃghātanipātaśītalāḥ śaśāṅkabhābhiḥ śiśirīkṛtāḥ punaḥ
      made cold by the falling of dew, and colder still by the light of the moon,

      and skies

      विपाण्डुतारागणचारुभूषणा
      vipāṇḍutārāgaṇacārubhūṣaṇā
      beautifully adorned by constellations as white as snow.

    • Much of the chapter is an erotic (but heteronormative) description of lovers taking refuge from the long, cold winter nights in the warmth of each other's bodies. It also describes śiśira as...

      प्रियजनरहितानां चितसंतापहेतुः
      priyajanarahitānāṃ citasaṃtāpahetuḥ
      the cause for the burning of the hearts of those without lovers.

      It goes to show that "cuffing season" and "winter cuddle buddies" are timeless, placeless phenomena.

  • The Viṣṇusahasranāma (विष्णुसहस्रनाम) is a Vaishnavite text listing a thousand names for Viṣṇu (विष्णु). The nominative singular śiśiraḥ occurs as name number 913 in this text.

    The original text is just a list of names with no commentary. Later commentators over the centuries have provided their own unique commentaries for why each of the thousand names is used as a name for Viṣṇu. For the name śiśiraḥ, the gist of the commentaries that I've seen20 seems to be that Viṣṇu is seen as providing cooling relief to those that are burning (in worldly desires, in suffering, etc).

Nickname

Backstory

I was born in India a few weeks before my due date, and my dad was not in India at the time. It took him a couple of days to get to India after I was born. My parents had not settled on a name for me before I was born: they only settled on Shishir after my dad got to India. In the intervening days, my family wanted to have some name to refer to me by, and decided on Sunny.

The name has stuck ever since: almost everyone in my family invariably calls me Sunny, and some people have even been surprised to learn that my legal name is not actually Sunny.

I realized very soon after immigrating to the United States that Sunny was much easier for Americans to pronounce than Shishir, and I started telling my teachers, classmates, friends, students, etc, that they could call me Sunny. Most do.

Pronunciation and orthography

The English pronunciation is just [ˈsʌ.ni].

The Hindustani pronunciation is usually the almost identical [səniː], though some Hindustani speakers over-faithfully preserve the English orthography in their pronunciation and geminate the "n," saying [sənniː]. Accordingly, the Nagari orthography is usually सनी but occasionally सन्नी. In the Urdu alphabet, it is س‬ن‬ی‬.

In Japanese, I like writing サニ.
