Schwa Deletion in Hindustani (2018-07-13)

Three kinds of “schwa deletion”

The phrase schwa deletion (or sometimes, schwa syncope) is used to refer to at least three different phenomena in the literature relating to Hindustani linguistics (as well as that of other Indo-Aryan languages). It is worth clearly disambiguating between the three.

Orthographic schwa deletion

The first phenomenon is what we might call orthographic schwa deletion. This is a purely orthographic phenomenon that arises when Nagari is used to transcribe Hindustani. The full consonant symbols of Nagari generally carry an inherent schwa, silencing which generally requires orthographic indication (by means of a virama or a half consonant symbol), but this is not consistently the case. In some words, an inherent schwa is silenced despite never being indicated in the orthography (eg, सड़कें, धड़कन, …), and in other words, two different forms are concurrent because of this ambiguity (eg, गर्मी, गरमी).

The literature on explaining this phenomenon abounds, for a practical reason. Nagari is very close to being a phonemic writing system for Hindustani, but it isn’t quite, and orthographic schwa deletion is one of the primary obstructions. Explaining orthographic schwa deletion would therefore permit very effective text-to-speech systems.

That said, it seems to be well understood that there is no purely orthographic condition which explains schwa deletion. Underlying orthographic schwa deletion seem to be certain diachronic factors as well as certain synchronic morphophonemic factors, without which orthographic schwa deletion cannot be completely explained. For example, the words धड़कने and धड़कनें are quite similar from a superficial orthographic standpoint, but there is no schwa deletion in the first (which has surface representation [dʱəɽəkneː]), while there is in the second (realized as [dʱəɽkənẽː]).

Diachronic schwa deletion

Second, there is what might be called diachronic schwa deletion. This involves comparing modern Hindustani to its ancestral languages and noting that certain schwas that existed in the phonemic representations of words in these ancestral languages have been silenced in modern Hindustani. For example, the word /hatʰ/ (“hand”) in modern Hindustani descends from a root that contained a final schwa (cf. the root hasta in Sanskrit).

Diachronic phenomena are always difficult to explain, due to an absence of completely accurate phonological data prior to the era of audio recordings. That said, it has been suggested that word-final schwas began to be systematically deleted at some point in the transition between the 1600s and today [1 sec. 6.3]. In fact, more seems to be true: not only word-final but morpheme-final schwas seem to have disappeared at some point.

Morphophonemic schwa deletion

Third, there is a synchronic phenomenon in modern Hindustani that we might call morphophonemic schwa deletion, which explains how schwas in the phonemic representation of one word are deleted in the phonemic representation of a morphologically derivative word. For an example of this phenomenon, consider the word /səɽək/ (“street”). Feminine nouns in the morphological class of /səɽək/ are put into the direct plural form by suffixation of /ẽ/, but the surface representation of the direct plural in this particular case is [səɽkẽː]. In other words, the second /ə/ in the phonemic representation /səɽək/ disappears in the surface representation of the plural.

The schwa in synchronic Hindustani morphophonemics

It seems to me that there are two principles to bear in mind regarding the schwa as it relates to synchronic Hindustani morphophonemics.

  • (Absence of morpheme-final schwas) No Hindustani morpheme ends in a schwa, except in a few tatsama Sanskrit loans where it is forced by phonotactic constraints.
  • (Morphophonemic schwa deletion) Subject to phonotactic constraints, schwas occurring in sequences of the form V₁C₁əC₂#V₂ are deleted, where the Vᵢ denote vowels, the Cᵢ denote consonant clusters, and # is a morpheme boundary.

The first principle should be relatively clear, though it may be worth saying a few words about the treatment of tatsama loans from Sanskrit into Hindustani. The general principle regarding these loans is that morpheme-final schwas are silenced. Sometimes, however, doing this would force a violation of phonotactic constraints. For example, consider the Sanskrit suffix -iya (“worth…”). Silencing the schwa would result in a suffix that forces /j/ to function as a syllable coda, which is phonotactically prohibited for most speakers. So instead, the Sanskrit suffix is loaned into Hindustani as a derivational suffix with a morpheme-final schwa as /ijə/, and shows up in words like /t͡ʃɪntənijə/ (“worth thinking about”).

It is the second principle, morphophonemic schwa deletion, that explains the deleted schwa in going from /səɽək/ to the surface representation of the direct plural [səɽkẽː]. Morphologically, the plural can be broken down as /səɽək#ẽ/, where /ẽ/ is an inflectional affix indicating pluralization for one of the two morphological classes of feminine nouns. This matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion, and we obtain the expected surface representation.

Morphophonemic schwa deletion also applies when the morpheme boundary involves a derivational affix. Consider for example the derivational affix /i/ which suffixes onto adjectives to produce nouns. When suffixed onto the morpheme /gəɾəm/ (“hot”), we obtain /gəɾəm#i/, which matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion and we obtain the expected surface representation [gəɾmiː] (“heat”).

For another example, consider the derivational affix /ən/ which suffixes onto verbal roots and produces nouns. When suffixed onto the verbal root /dʱəɽək/ (“to beat,” as in what a heart does), we obtain /dʱəɽək#ən/, which again matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion and we obtain the expected surface representation [dʱəɽkən] (“heartbeat”).

Using the second example above, we can also see the role that phonotactics plays in restricting the application of morphophonemic schwa deletion. If we suffix the pluralizing inflectional affix /ẽ/ onto /dʱəɽkən/, we obtain /dʱəɽkən#ẽ/. This again matches the form V₁C₁əC₂#V₂ with C₁ being the cluster /ɽk/ and C₂ being /n/, but applying schwa deletion would violate phonotactic constraints of Hindustani: /ɽk/ is not a valid coda, and /kn/ is not a valid onset, so there would be no possible syllabification satisfying phonotactic constraints. Thus morphophonemic schwa deletion does not operate at this point, and we obtain the expected surface representation [dʱəɽkənẽː].

It may also be worth observing the comparison with the oblique infinitive [dʱəɽəkneː]. The morphological breakdown here is /dʱəɽək#ne/, with the inflectional affix /ne/ indicating an oblique infinitive being suffixed onto the verbal root /dʱəɽək/. This does not match the form required by morphophonemic schwa deletion, and indeed no schwa is deleted in the surface representation.

Another example worth discussing are the words [ləɽkaː] (“boy”), [ləɽkiː], (“girl”), [ləɽəkpən] (“childhood”), etc. Based on these examples, it may make sense to posit the existence of a bound morpheme /ləɽək/ in Hindustani, a nominal morpheme with rough meaning “child.” It is definitely a bound morpheme, since it does not occur in isolation. Affixing this with the singular direct masculine morpheme /aː/ yields /ləɽək#a/, to which the morphophonemic schwa deletion rule applies, yielding the expected surface representation [ləɽkaː]. On the other hand, affixing the derivational morpheme /pən/ (which turns nominal morphemes X into nouns meaning “the attribute of being X”) yields /ləɽək#pən/, and morphophonemic schwa deletion does not apply, again yielding the expected surface representation.

A fourth kind of schwa deletion…?

There are some morphemes in Hindustani in which the (dis)appearance of a schwa varies idiosyncratically. Examples abound: [dʱəɾəm] vs [dʱəɾm] (“dharma”), [ʃəɾəm] vs [ʃəɾm] (“shyness, shame”), [kətəl] vs [kətl] (“murder”), [gəɾəm] vs [gəɾm] (“hot”), [məɾəz] vs [mərz] (“disease”), [kʰətəm] vs [kʰətm], etc.

There are two possible diaphonemic explanations one might propose for this phenomenon.

  • One involves a diaphonemic representation of words like this in which the schwa is absent (eg, //dʱəɾm//), and then a schwa insertion rule to give rise to the phonemic representation for speakers whose phonotactic constraints do not permit the necessary clusters to realize such diaphonemic representations (eg, for speakers for whom /ɾm/ is not a valid syllable coda, we get //dʱəɾm// → /dʱəɾəm/).
  • The second possibility is a diaphonemic representation that includes the schwa (eg, //dʱəɾəm//), and then a schwa deletion rule that gives rise to the phonemic representation of speakers for whom the necessary clusters are phonotactically valid (eg, for speakers for whom /ɾm/ is valid syllable coda, we get //dʱəɾəm// → /dʱəɾm/).

It seems to me that it may be the second option that more reliably models Hindustani diaphonemics. Consider, for example, the case of the surface representations [məɾəz] vs [mərz] (“disease”). The word ultimately derives from Arabic /maradˤ/. The short /a/ and the /dˤ/ of Arabic are systematically turned into /ə/ and //z// in Hindustani, respectively. With the schwa insertion model, it would be entirely mysterious why Arabic /maradˤ/ yields the Hindustani diaphonemic representation //məɾz// which some speakers then manage to “correct” back to //məɾəz//, especially since it is usually the highly educated that have more permissive phonotactic constraints when it comes to consonant clusters.

On the other hand, with the schwa deletion model, we would have a diaphonemic //məɾəz//, and the transformation to //məɾz// is explained by hypercorrection, due perhaps to a perceived analogy with similar words of Perso-Arabic origin such as //qarz// (“debt”).

This, then, suggests a possible fourth kind of schwa deletion, one which we might call diaphonemic schwa deletion. A possible formalization may be that a morpheme-final sequence VC₁əC₂ in the diaphonemic representation of a Hindustani morpheme becomes VC₁C₂ in the phonemic representations of speakers for whom C₁C₂ is a phonotactically valid cluster.

Note that the phenomenon of morphophonemic schwa deletion described earlier occurs within the speech of individual speakers; in contrast, the diaphonemic schwa deletion phenomenon just described compares speech of different speakers. Diaphonemics seems to have tenuous biological/psychological reality, so if diaphonemic schwa deletion is anything at all, it is merely a convenient abstraction that may help describe the dialectical and idiolectical variation regarding schwas in certain settings.

Relation to orthographic schwa deletion

Using what we’ve discussed above, we can formulate some vague observations about Nagari orthographic conventions as it relates to schwas. The first observation regarding Nagari orthographic conventions is related to the absence of morpheme-final schwas in Hindustani.

  • Morpheme-final consonant phonemes are indicated with full consonant symbols. Conversely, the inherent schwa of a full consonant symbol that would be morpheme-final is deleted in the phonemic representation, when this is phonotactically valid.

The first statement should be relatively clear, though it does require a small caveat: some authors occasionally choose to spell some tatsama loans with a word-final virama when Sanskrit orthographic conventions would require this virama. For example, it is relatively standard to transcribe /d͡ʒəgət/ (“world”) as जगत, though some authors occasionally will write जगत्.

This convention also explains the convention of transcribing multi-morpheme words like /pən#t͡ʃəkki/ (“waterwheel”) as पनचक्की, and /ʊp#vas/ (“fast,” as in religiously-inflicted hunger) as उपवास. It may be worth noting here that the Hindustani prefix /ʊp/ derives from the Sanskrit prefix upa via the diachronic schwa deletion process described earlier.

Next up, we have some observations regarding schwas deleted via morphophonemic schwa deletion. If a schwa is deleted in pronunciation via morphophonemic schwa deletion in the process of attaching…

  • … an inflectional affix, the deleted schwa is never indicated in the orthography (eg, one always write सड़कें and never *सड़्कें).
  • … a derivational affix, the situation is ambiguous: some words will never be marked for the deleted schwa (eg, धड़कन), others will relatively consistently be marked, and some will have two orthographic variants (one marking the deleted schwa, as in गर्मी, and the other not, as in गरमी).

The situation is also ambiguous when it comes to diaphonemic schwa deletion.

  • If a schwa is deleted in the process of diaphonemic schwa deletion, sometimes the deleted schwa is somewhat consistently indicated in the orthography (eg, धर्म is quite common but धरम is increasingly uncommon), and other times there are two orthographic variants (eg, गर्म and गरम both occur relatively frequently).

A final observation is that orthographic schwa deletion occasionally occurs when none of the factors described above would suffice as adequate explanations. For example, the morpheme /ət͡ʃɾəd͡ʒ/ (“astonishment”) is consistently transcribed in Nagari as अचरज, without marking the absent medial schwa. An etymology deriving it from Sanskrit āścarya may explain why the /t͡ʃ/ in /ət͡ʃɾəd͡ʒ/ should be written with a full consonant symbol, but /ət͡ʃ/ in Hindustani and āśca in Sanskrit are not morphemes as far as I know, so this does not seem to be a particular case of the diachronic morpheme-final schwa deletion rule I’ve described above. It may be that there are other diachronic schwa deletion rules that can be formalized, or it may just be that this is an orthographic anomaly. Ultimately, orthographic schwa deletion seems to be an amalgam resulting from from some diachronic etymological factors, some synchronic morphophonemic factors, and some idiosyncratic factors depending on the personal preferences of the scribe.
