Schwa Deletion in Hindustani (2018-07-13)

A discussion of several kinds of deleted schwas

Three kinds of вАЬschwa deletionвАЭ

The phrase schwa deletion (or sometimes, schwa syncope) is used to refer to at least three different phenomena in the literature relating to Hindustani linguistics (as well as that of other Indo-Aryan languages). It is worth clearly disambiguating between the three.

Orthographic schwa deletion

The first phenomenon is what we might call orthographic schwa deletion. This is a purely orthographic phenomenon that arises when Nagari is used to transcribe Hindustani. The full consonant symbols of Nagari generally carry an inherent schwa, silencing which generally requires orthographic indication (by means of a virama or a half consonant symbol), but this is not consistently the case. In some words, an inherent schwa is silenced despite never being indicated in the orthography (eg, а§Єа•Ьа§Ха•За§В, а§Іа•Ьа§Ха§®, вА¶), and in other words, two different forms are concurrent because of this ambiguity (eg, а§Ча§∞а•На§Ѓа•А, а§Ча§∞а§Ѓа•А).

The literature on explaining this phenomenon abounds, for a practical reason. Nagari is very close to being a phonemic writing system for Hindustani, but it isnвАЩt quite, and orthographic schwa deletion is one of the primary obstructions. Explaining orthographic schwa deletion would therefore permit very effective text-to-speech systems.

That said, it seems to be well understood that there is no purely orthographic condition which explains schwa deletion. Underlying orthographic schwa deletion seem to be certain diachronic factors as well as certain synchronic morphophonemic factors, without which orthographic schwa deletion cannot be completely explained. For example, the words а§Іа•Ьа§Ха§®а•З and а§Іа•Ьа§Ха§®а•За§В are quite similar from a superficial orthographic standpoint, but there is no schwa deletion in the first (which has surface representation [d ±…Щ…љ…ЩkneЋР]), while there is in the second (realized as [d ±…Щ…љk…ЩnбЇљЋР]).

Diachronic schwa deletion

Second, there is what might be called diachronic schwa deletion. This involves comparing modern Hindustani to its ancestral languages and noting that certain schwas that existed in the phonemic representations of words in these ancestral languages have been silenced in modern Hindustani. For example, the word /hat ∞/ (вАЬhandвАЭ) in modern Hindustani descends from a root that contained a final schwa (cf. the root hasta in Sanskrit).

Diachronic phenomena are always difficult to explain, due to an absence of completely accurate phonological data prior to the era of audio recordings. That said, it has been suggested that word-final schwas began to be systematically deleted at some point in the transition between the 1600s and today [@misra, section 6.3]. In fact, more seems to be true: not only word-final but morpheme-final schwas seem to have disappeared at some point.

Morphophonemic schwa deletion

Third, there is a synchronic phenomenon in modern Hindustani that we might call morphophonemic schwa deletion, which explains how schwas in the phonemic representation of one word are deleted in the phonemic representation of a morphologically derivative word. For an example of this phenomenon, consider the word /s…Щ…љ…Щk/ (вАЬstreetвАЭ). Feminine nouns in the morphological class of /s…Щ…љ…Щk/ are put into the direct plural form by suffixation of /бЇљ/, but the surface representation of the direct plural in this particular case is [s…Щ…љkбЇљЋР]. In other words, the second /…Щ/ in the phonemic representation /s…Щ…љ…Щk/ disappears in the surface representation of the plural.

The schwa in synchronic Hindustani morphophonemics

It seems to me that there are two principles to bear in mind regarding the schwa as it relates to synchronic Hindustani morphophonemics.

  • (Absence of morpheme-final schwas) No Hindustani morpheme ends in a schwa, except in a few tatsama Sanskrit loans where it is forced by phonotactic constraints.
  • (Morphophonemic schwa deletion) Subject to phonotactic constraints, schwas occurring in sequences of the form VвВБCвВБ…ЩCвВВ#VвВВ are deleted, where the VбµҐ denote vowels, the CбµҐ denote consonant clusters, and # is a morpheme boundary.

The first principle should be relatively clear, though it may be worth saying a few words about the treatment of tatsama loans from Sanskrit into Hindustani. The general principle regarding these loans is that morpheme-final schwas are silenced. Sometimes, however, doing this would force a violation of phonotactic constraints. For example, consider the Sanskrit suffix -iya (вАЬworthвА¶вАЭ). Silencing the schwa would result in a suffix that forces /j/ to function as a syllable coda, which is phonotactically prohibited for most speakers. So instead, the Sanskrit suffix is loaned into Hindustani as a derivational suffix with a morpheme-final schwa as /ij…Щ/, and shows up in words like /tЌ° Г…™nt…Щnij…Щ/ (вАЬworth thinking aboutвАЭ).

It is the second principle, morphophonemic schwa deletion, that explains the deleted schwa in going from /s…Щ…љ…Щk/ to the surface representation of the direct plural [s…Щ…љkбЇљЋР]. Morphologically, the plural can be broken down as /s…Щ…љ…Щk#бЇљ/, where /бЇљ/ is an inflectional affix indicating pluralization for one of the two morphological classes of feminine nouns. This matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion, and we obtain the expected surface representation.

Morphophonemic schwa deletion also applies when the morpheme boundary involves a derivational affix. Consider for example the derivational affix /i/ which suffixes onto adjectives to produce nouns. When suffixed onto the morpheme /g…Щ…Њ…Щm/ (вАЬhotвАЭ), we obtain /g…Щ…Њ…Щm#i/, which matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion and we obtain the expected surface representation [g…Щ…ЊmiЋР] (вАЬheatвАЭ).

For another example, consider the derivational affix /…Щn/ which suffixes onto verbal roots and produces nouns. When suffixed onto the verbal root /d ±…Щ…љ…Щk/ (вАЬto beat,вАЭ as in what a heart does), we obtain /d ±…Щ…љ…Щk#…Щn/, which again matches the conditions for morphophonemic schwa deletion and we obtain the expected surface representation [d ±…Щ…љk…Щn] (вАЬheartbeatвАЭ).

Using the second example above, we can also see the role that phonotactics plays in restricting the application of morphophonemic schwa deletion. If we suffix the pluralizing inflectional affix /бЇљ/ onto /d ±…Щ…љk…Щn/, we obtain /d ±…Щ…љk…Щn#бЇљ/. This again matches the form VвВБCвВБ…ЩCвВВ#VвВВ with CвВБ being the cluster /…љk/ and CвВВ being /n/, but applying schwa deletion would violate phonotactic constraints of Hindustani: /…љk/ is not a valid coda, and /kn/ is not a valid onset, so there would be no possible syllabification satisfying phonotactic constraints. Thus morphophonemic schwa deletion does not operate at this point, and we obtain the expected surface representation [d ±…Щ…љk…ЩnбЇљЋР].

It may also be worth observing the comparison with the oblique infinitive [d ±…Щ…љ…ЩkneЋР]. The morphological breakdown here is /d ±…Щ…љ…Щk#ne/, with the inflectional affix /ne/ indicating an oblique infinitive being suffixed onto the verbal root /d ±…Щ…љ…Щk/. This does not match the form required by morphophonemic schwa deletion, and indeed no schwa is deleted in the surface representation.

Another example worth discussing are the words [l…Щ…љkaЋР] (вАЬboyвАЭ), [l…Щ…љkiЋР], (вАЬgirlвАЭ), [l…Щ…љ…Щkp…Щn] (вАЬchildhoodвАЭ), etc. Based on these examples, it may make sense to posit the existence of a bound morpheme /l…Щ…љ…Щk/ in Hindustani, a nominal morpheme with rough meaning вАЬchild.вАЭ It is definitely a bound morpheme, since it does not occur in isolation. Affixing this with the singular direct masculine morpheme /aЋР/ yields /l…Щ…љ…Щk#a/, to which the morphophonemic schwa deletion rule applies, yielding the expected surface representation [l…Щ…љkaЋР]. On the other hand, affixing the derivational morpheme /p…Щn/ (which turns nominal morphemes X into nouns meaning вАЬthe attribute of being XвАЭ) yields /l…Щ…љ…Щk#p…Щn/, and morphophonemic schwa deletion does not apply, again yielding the expected surface representation.

A fourth kind of schwa deletionвА¶?

There are some morphemes in Hindustani in which the (dis)appearance of a schwa varies idiosyncratically. Examples abound: [d ±…Щ…Њ…Щm] vs [d ±…Щ…Њm] (вАЬdharmaвАЭ), [ Г…Щ…Њ…Щm] vs [ Г…Щ…Њm] (вАЬshyness, shameвАЭ), [k…Щt…Щl] vs [k…Щtl] (вАЬmurderвАЭ), [g…Щ…Њ…Щm] vs [g…Щ…Њm] (вАЬhotвАЭ), [m…Щ…Њ…Щz] vs [m…Щrz] (вАЬdiseaseвАЭ), [k ∞…Щt…Щm] vs [k ∞…Щtm], etc.

There are two possible diaphonemic explanations one might propose for this phenomenon.

  • One involves a diaphonemic representation of words like this in which the schwa is absent (eg, //d ±…Щ…Њm//), and then a schwa insertion rule to give rise to the phonemic representation for speakers whose phonotactic constraints do not permit the necessary clusters to realize such diaphonemic representations (eg, for speakers for whom /…Њm/ is not a valid syllable code, we get //d ±…Щ…Њm// вЖТ/d ±…Щ…Њ…Щm/).
  • The second possibility is a diaphonemic representation that includes the schwa (eg, //d ±…Щ…Њ…Щm//), and then a schwa deletion rule that gives rise to the phonemic representation of speakers for whom the necessary clusters are phonotactically valid (eg, for speakers for whom /…Њm/ is valid syllable code, we get //d ±…Щ…Њ…Щm// вЖТ /d ±…Щ…Њm/).

It seems to me that it may be the second option that more reliably models Hindustani diaphonemics. Consider, for example, the case of the surface representations [m…Щ…Њ…Щz] vs [m…Щrz] (вАЬdiseaseвАЭ). The word ultimately derives from Arabic /maradЋ§/. The short /a/ and the /dЋ§/ of Arabic are systematically turned into /…Щ/ and //z// in Hindustani, respectively. With the schwa insertion model, it would be entirely mysterious why Arabic /maradЋ§/ yields the Hindustani diaphonemic representation //m…Щ…Њz// which some speakers then manage to вАЬcorrectвАЭ back to //m…Щ…Њ…Щz//, especially since it is usually the highly educated that have more permissive phonotactic constraints when it comes to consonant clusters.

On the other hand, with the schwa deletion model, we would have a diaphonemic //m…Щ…Њ…Щz//, and the transformation to //m…Щ…Њz// is explained by hypercorrection (perhaps due to a percieved analogy with similar words of Perso-Arabic origin, such as //qarz// (вАЬdebtвАЭ)).

This, then, suggests a possible fourth kind of schwa deletion, one which we might call diaphonemic schwa deletion. A possible formalization may be that a morpheme-final sequence VCвВБ…ЩCвВВ in the diaphonemic representation of a Hindustani morpheme becomes VCвВБCвВВ in the phonemic representations of speakers for whom CвВБCвВВ is a phonotactically valid cluster.

Diaphonemics seems to have tenuous biological/psychological reality, so it is worth noting that diaphonemic schwa deletion is distinct from morphophonemic schwa deletion in that the latter is describing a genuine linguistic phenomenon. If diaphonemic schwa deletion is anything at all, it would merely be a convenient abstraction that may help describe the dialectical and idiolectical variation regarding schwas in certain settings.

Relation to orthographic schwa deletion

Using what weвАЩve discussed above, we can formulate some vague observations about Nagari orthographic conventions as it relates to schwas. The first observation regarding Nagari orthographic conventions is related to the absence of morpheme-final schwas in Hindustani.

  • Morpheme-final consonant phonemes are indicated with full consonant symbols. Conversely, the inherent schwa of a full consonant symbol that would be morpheme-final is deleted in the phonemic representation, when this is phonotactically valid.

The first statement should be relatively clear, though it does require a small caveat: some authors occasionally choose to spell some tatsama loans with a word-final virama when Sanskrit orthographic conventions would require this virama. For example, it is relatively standard to transcribe /dЌ° Т…Щg…Щt/ (вАЬworldвАЭ) as а§Ьа§Ча§§, though some authors occasionally will write а§Ьа§Ча§§а•Н.

The situation is also ambiguous when it comes to diaphonemic schwa deletion.

  • If a schwa is deleted in the process of diaphonemic schwa deletion, sometimes the deleted schwa is somewhat consistently indicated in the orthography (eg, а§Іа§∞а•На§Ѓ is quite common but а§Іа§∞а§Ѓ is increasingly uncommon), and other times there are two orthographic variants (eg, а§Ча§∞а•На§Ѓ and а§Ча§∞а§Ѓ both occur relatively frequently).

A final observation is that orthographic schwa deletion occasionally occurs when none of the factors described above would suffice as adequate explanations. For example, the morpheme /…ЩtЌ° Г…Њ…ЩdЌ° Т/ (вАЬastonishmentвАЭ) is consistently transcribed in Nagari as а§Еа§Ъа§∞а§Ь, without marking the absent medial schwa. An etymology deriving it from Sanskrit ƒБ≈Ыcarya may explain why the /tЌ° Г/ in /…ЩtЌ° Г…Њ…ЩdЌ° Т/ should be written with a full consonant symbol, but /…ЩtЌ° Г/ in Hindustani and ƒБ≈Ыca in Sanskrit are not morphemes as far as I know, so this does not seem to be a particular case of the diachronic morpheme-final schwa deletion rule IвАЩve described above. It may be that there are other diachronic schwa deletion rules that can be formalized, or it may just be that this is an orthographic anomaly. Ultimately, orthographic schwa deletion seems to be an amalgam resulting from from some diachronic etymological factors, some synchronic morphophonemic factors, and some idiosyncratic factors depending on the personal preferences of the scribe.
