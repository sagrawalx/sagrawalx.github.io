Geography and Climate

Playa Del Carmen is a city on the Caribbean coast of Mexico, about 34 mi (55 km) south of Cancun International Airport (CUN). The city is located in the Riviera Maya tourist district, in the state of Quintana Roo, on the Yucatán peninsula.

The region has a tropical savannah climate. In April and May, temperatures in Playa Del Carmen typically vary between 70–89°F (21–32°C). This is also a relatively dry time of year: April and May each average 3–7 rainy days with 2–3 in (51–78 mm) of rainfall. The sea is also warm: water temperatures are roughly 81–82°F (27–28°C).

Travel Documentation

All of you will need an unexpired passport to travel to Mexico. If you’re a citizen of the US or you have a US visa, you shouldn’t need anything else.

All of you will be asked to fill out a form called the FMM (“Forma Migratoria Multiple,” or Multiple Immigration Form) on your flight. This is not a visa. Apparently, when you arrive in Mexico, an immigration official at the airport will stamp two parts of this document and give you the smaller part. You’ll need to keep this part safe and surrender it when you leave Mexico. If you lose it, there might be a fine.

Transportation

You’ll want to fly to Cancun International Airport (CUN).

You can take a bus, shuttle, or taxi to go between the airport and Playa Del Carmen.

If you have a US driver’s license, you can also rent a car. You can rent one at the airport for the duration of your stay (eg, at City Car Rental), or you can rent one when you need it from one of several car rental agencies in PDC (eg, the Avis next door to the hotel). Parking at the hotel costs 100 MXN (~ 5 USD) per night. If you rent a car, note that you will probably need to purchase Third Party Liability insurance through the car rental agency since Mexican law requires you to have this through a Mexican insurer. You are also required to have Collision Damage insurance, but your US-based credit card might provide this. If it does, and if you intend to decline Collision Damage through the rental agency, make sure to carry documentation.

Valladoid is a city en route between Chichen Itza and PDC that is famous for its Mayan food, so we thought we could stop there for lunch after visiting Chichen Itza on our way back to PDC (maybe at IX CAT IK?). We haven’t made any advance bookings for Chichen Itza or Valladoid, but Tuesday seems like a reasonable day to do this.

There should be a variety of marine life around, including sea turtles and stingrays! On board, there’ll be an open bar (margaritas, beer, wine, etc), snacks (chips, guac, ceviche), snorkel gear, floaties, towels, etc. We’ll meet at a dock in Puerto Aventuras (26 minutes south of PDC) at 8:15am.

  • Fri 4/29: Xplor. Xplor is an adventure park in PDC with zip lines, ATVs, flooded caverns, and fun things like that. If you want to go with us, you can book tickets through their website. There’s a discount for booking in advance. Children under 5 can’t do any of the activities, but they can be inside the park.

Covid-19

As of March 19, Mexico has administered enough doses of Covid-19 vaccines for 73.7% of the population to receive two doses each. That number is currently growing by 10% every 64 days. For the latest, see Reuters’ global coronavirus tracker and specifically their Mexico tracker.

As of March 19, we are still required to show a negative Covid test result before boarding our flights back to the US. The hotel reception will be able to help us arrange a Covid test in town. Alternatively, you can get an antigen test at Cancun Airport for 290 MXN (~ 15 USD). Results of this test should be available in 30–60 minutes.

Miscellaneous

You’ll certainly want to bring swim and/or beach clothes! If you bring sunscreen with you, please make sure that it’s biodegradable (non-biodegradable sunscreen destroys coral reefs and is banned in much of the area).

You might also check to see if your cell phone plan will provide coverage in Mexico. Sunny and Markus will be able to text and use data, and also make calls for 0.20 USD per minute.

As of March 19, the currency conversion is about 21 MXN (pesos) per USD. Note that Mexico prefixes prices in pesos using the $ symbol (just like the US does for prices in dollars).
